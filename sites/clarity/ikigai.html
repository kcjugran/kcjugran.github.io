How to Find Your Ikigai

Ikigai is a Japanese concept meaning "reason for being." This is a practical guide to the four-circle framework and the questions that reveal the pursuit sitting at the centre of your life.

By Karan Jugran, Living Foundations · Part of the Clarity series

Most advice about "finding your purpose" is vague. Ikigai is useful because it is specific: it asks you to look at your life from four different angles and then find where those angles overlap. That overlap is your reason for being — the thing worth getting out of bed for.

The four circles of Ikigai

The framework breaks life purpose into four questions. Answer each one honestly, on its own, before you look for connections.

Circle 1

What you love

Activities where time disappears. Pure joy — nothing to do with how skilled you are.

Circle 2

What you're good at

Natural strengths or built skills. People notice. You feel genuinely confident here.

Circle 3

What the world needs

Problems that bother you deeply. Gaps you see that others seem to ignore.

Circle 4

What you can be paid for

Skills with real market value — now, or with time invested.

Where the circles overlap

The magic of Ikigai isn't in any single circle — it's in the overlaps. Each pair of circles produces something meaningful, and each has a blind spot when it's missing a fourth ingredient:

  • Love + Good at = Passion. Fulfilling, but may not pay or serve others.
  • Good at + Paid for = Profession. Comfortable, but can feel empty without love or mission.
  • Paid for + World needs = Vocation. Useful and viable, but draining if you don't love it.
  • Love + World needs = Mission. Deeply meaningful, but unstable without income or skill.

Your goal is to find the one pursuit that sits at the centre of all four circles. It rarely appears fully formed — you assemble it by being honest about each circle first, then noticing what keeps showing up across them.

How to actually do the exercise

This is a one-time deep exercise. Set aside 45 minutes to 2 hours and do it with full focus. For each circle, don't stop at your first answer — aim for three to five, because the deeper answers are where the real insight lives.

A few of the most revealing prompts from the Clarity series:

  • What are 3–5 activities — from childhood or now — where time completely disappeared for you?
  • What do people consistently ask you for help with? What problem do they bring to you?
  • What are the 3–5 biggest problems you see in the world today, and why does each bother you more than others?
  • What 3–5 skills do you have where real money feels possible with a bit more time or guidance?

When you finish, look across all your answers for patterns. The pursuit that touches all four circles — something you love, are good at, that the world needs, and that you can be paid for — is the direction to walk in.

Frequently asked questions

What is Ikigai?

Ikigai is a Japanese concept meaning "reason for being." A popular framework breaks life purpose into four overlapping circles — what you love, what you're good at, what the world needs, and what you can be paid for. Where all four meet is your Ikigai.

What are the four circles of Ikigai?

What you love, what you're good at, what the world needs, and what you can be paid for. Their overlaps form passion, profession, vocation, and mission.

How long does the Ikigai exercise take?

It's a one-time deep exercise that usually takes 45 minutes to 2 hours. Answer the questions for each circle honestly and with full focus rather than rushing.

Is Ikigai really about finding one perfect job?

No. Ikigai is a lens for aligning your life, not a single job title. The goal is pursuits near the centre of all four circles — and your Ikigai can evolve as you and the world change.
