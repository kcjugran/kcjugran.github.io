# Five Simple Stretches to Do in Bed for Better Sleep

Ever wake up and feel like your whole body is locked up? Tight lower back, a neck and shoulders that have been aching for hours, that general sense of "my body just isn't right"? A lot of that stiffness isn't random — it builds up over the day and then gets carried straight into bed with you, where it quietly affects how well you actually sleep.

The good news is you don't need a mat, a studio, or even to get out of bed to do something about it. This routine is built around five stretches you can do lying down, right before you fall asleep. None of them require you to stand up or leave the comfort of your sheets — you just shift position in bed. Done regularly, they help your body properly relax before sleep, which in turn tends to improve how deep and comfortable that sleep is.

The five areas this routine targets are the ones that tend to get tight from a day of sitting, working, and general daily tension: the neck, the chest, and the lower back. Here's how to work through them.

## 1. Neck Side Stretch

Lying on your back, gently reach one arm up and over to the opposite side, letting your head tilt to follow the pull. Hold wherever feels appropriately tight for you — everyone's flexibility is different, so let your own comfort be the guide rather than forcing a specific range. This is a simple, effective stretch for releasing tension that builds up along the side of the neck, an area that carries a lot of hidden tightness by the end of the day.

## 2. Lying Spinal Rotation

This one works your lower back and the sides of your torso. Lying on your back, keep one arm extended out to the side, then rotate your lower body — hips and legs — to the opposite side while keeping your shoulders grounded. Let your legs relax into the twist rather than actively pushing. You'll feel a long stretch running through your lower back and the entire side of your body. How far your legs rotate depends entirely on where you're holding tightness that day — go only as far as feels good, not as far as you think you "should."

## 3. Seated Side Reach

Sitting up in bed, extend one leg out, bend the other in for support, and reach toward your extended foot — going as far as feels natural, whether that's just past your knee or all the way to your toes. The goal isn't to force your torso flat over your leg; it's to gently work against your own resistance, letting the stretch build gradually along the side of your body and neck. If there's any strain in your neck during this one, resting your head on your arm like a small pillow takes the pressure off while you still get the benefit through your side and back.

## 4. Shoulder and Upper Back Rotation

For this one, lie on your side, extend one arm out, and use the position of your legs and hips to anchor your lower body while you rotate your upper body and shoulder toward the opposite side. Done well, your shoulder, chest, and the arm on the same side end up in roughly one line — you should feel a strong stretch running across your entire upper body as you open up that shoulder and chest area. This is particularly good for people who carry tension across the chest and shoulders from hours of sitting or hunching forward.

## 5. Reclined Butterfly Stretch

This is the classic one many people already know — soles of the feet brought together, knees relaxed open to the sides, heels drawn in as close to the body as feels comfortable. Once you're in position, let everything go loose and let gravity do the work rather than actively pressing your knees down. It's a deeply relaxing stretch for the inner thighs and hips, and it pairs naturally with slow, easy breathing — which makes it a good one to settle into right before you actually try to fall asleep.

There's also a lengthening stretch worth pairing with your neck work: gently tuck your chin, then use your hand at the back of your head to draw it upward while lengthening through the crown, feeling the stretch travel down the back and sides of your neck. This one effectively works both the front and back neck muscles together, along with the sides, so it's a nice way to round things out.

## How to Put It Together

You don't need a rigid protocol here. Spend roughly two to five minutes total, either timing yourself or simply counting slow, deep breaths — somewhere around twenty to thirty breaths per side works well if you're switching sides for the stretches that need it. Realistically, the whole sequence takes about three to four minutes once you include both sides.

**Takeaways:**

- Do this sequence lying in bed, right before you try to sleep — no need to get up or set up equipment.
- Move through each stretch slowly, following your own range of motion rather than pushing for a fixed depth.
- Use breath count (roughly 20–30 slow breaths per side) or a simple timer to pace yourself if you don't want to overthink it.
- If a stretch causes neck discomfort, modify it — resting your head on your arm is a simple fix that still lets you get the benefit.
- Consistency matters more than intensity: doing this most nights will do more for your sleep quality than doing it perfectly once.

Give it a few nights and pay attention to how your body feels both falling asleep and waking up. Small, consistent habits like this tend to add up in ways that are easy to underestimate until you actually stick with them.
