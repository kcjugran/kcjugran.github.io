# The Basics of Building an Exercise Program (Part 1)

Most people put exercises together the way they'd put together a shopping list — grab what feels good, do it in whatever order comes to mind. It works, for a while. But if you've ever wondered why some routines deliver more, session after session, while others feel like effort without payoff, the answer usually isn't the exercises themselves. It's the *structure* around them.

This article breaks down the framework for designing an exercise program — exercise order, how to think about reps and sets, and how to manage rest. It's part one of a series, so think of this as the foundation the next installment builds on.

## Why structure matters more than any single exercise

A textbook program and a program that works for a real person are two different things. Theory doesn't always survive contact with real life — clients get bored, bodies respond differently, and a "technically correct" program you can't stick with isn't actually good. The goal isn't to follow a rulebook perfectly; it's to understand the logic well enough to bend it intelligently. These are principles to reason with, not commandments.

## Start with a real warm-up

Before anything else, prepare the body — especially if you're dealing with tight shoulders, a stiff back, or hips that don't move well. Simple movements that stretch and gently activate the muscles you're about to train are enough. The point is to get blood moving and joints ready, not to exhaust yourself before you've started.

## The order of exercises: primary, secondary, and beyond

Once warmed up, the order you do things in matters more than most people assume.

**Primary exercises** come first — your big, demanding compound movements, where you can lift the most weight and your body is freshest. Bench press before dumbbell press, squat before lunges. Whichever version of a movement lets you handle the heaviest, most demanding load goes first, while you have full strength and focus available.

**Secondary exercises** follow — usually still compound movements, but slightly easier or lighter versions of the same pattern, like dumbbell press after bench press. There's a reason beyond "heavy stuff first": a barbell press lets you brace your whole body and share load across more muscle groups, so recovery is faster; a dumbbell press isolates effort into fewer muscles, fatiguing them more directly. Doing the more fatiguing version after the less fatiguing one protects performance across the session.

The same logic applies across *different* body parts. If your primary lift taxes a muscle a later secondary lift depends on, you'll tank that second exercise — a shoulder press after a chest press that already exhausted your shoulders and triceps will suffer. Pairing exercises that don't compete for the same fatigued muscles keeps every lift performing closer to its best.

**Tertiary work** — isolation exercises, finishing circuits, drop sets — comes last, where you can safely chase "the pump," target a specific muscle, or add stability work without compromising anything earlier.

There's also a place, right at the start (before primary lifts), for **speed or power movements** — medicine ball throws, jumps. These are trained fresh and never taken to failure, because the goal is teaching the body to fire fast and explosively. Once you're tired, that quality disappears first.

## Reps: different ranges, different goals

Rep ranges roughly map to what you're training for:

- **1–5 reps** leans toward pure strength, without building much size on its own.
- **6–30 reps** is the broad zone where muscle growth happens, with some strength benefit too.
- **20+ reps**, taken close to failure, still builds muscle — what drives growth isn't the weight on the bar, it's how close to failure you push the set. A tough 25-rep set and a tough 6-rep set can both build muscle if both are taken near their limit.

Upper and lower body behave differently. Legs are used to higher-repetition daily demand (walking, standing), so they tolerate — and need — more reps for the same effect upper body gets from fewer. A practical range is roughly 8–15 reps for upper body, a bit higher for lower body, adjusted by how close to failure you can safely push.

## Sets: how much is enough

A commonly cited target is around 10–20 sets per muscle group per week, split across training days. That's a guideline, not a hard rule — the right number depends on recovery capacity, how hard each set is pushed, and training frequency.

Too few sets and you leave progress on the table. Too many, without matching intensity, just adds fatigue without results. The variable that matters isn't set count alone; it's whether each set is trained with enough effort to matter.

## Tempo: control matters more than counting seconds

Don't obsess over lowering a weight for an exact number of seconds — control the weight. Momentum or jerking it around takes tension off the muscle into a wasted swing; dial that back rather than counting in your head. Two to three seconds on the controlled portion is a reasonable anchor, especially for beginners still learning coordination. As technique improves, exact tempo counting matters less. Control the rep — that's the real rule.

## Rest: don't fidget with it

Rest should scale with how demanding the set was — heavier, lower-rep work needs longer rest (a few minutes), lighter, higher-rep sets need less. More important: keep rest consistent week to week for a given exercise. Constantly changing rest time along with everything else means losing the ability to tell whether you're actually progressing or just training under different conditions. Change one variable at a time.

## Takeaway: what to actually do with this

- **Sequence deliberately**: warm-up, speed/power work, heaviest primary lift, lighter secondary version, then isolation/finishing work.
- **Pick rep ranges by goal** — lower for pure strength, moderate-to-higher for growth — remembering effort near failure matters as much as the number.
- **Aim for a reasonable weekly set volume per muscle group**, then adjust by recovery, not just paper math.
- **Control the weight rather than counting tempo seconds** — smooth, deliberate reps beat momentum.
- **Keep rest periods consistent** across sessions so you can actually track progress.

This is the "why" behind the order things go in. Part two goes further into applying this to real program design — if any of this raised questions about your own routine, hold onto them.
