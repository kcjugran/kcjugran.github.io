# What Is Ikigai? A Journal-Based Guide to Finding Your Life Purpose (Part 1)

If you've heard the word "ikigai" tossed around in wellness circles, you've probably seen the tidy four-circle diagram before you've ever sat down and actually worked through what it means for you. This video fixes that. It's the first of a three-part series built around one goal: a structured, question-by-question way to figure out your purpose, instead of just admiring the concept from a distance.

## Where the Concept Actually Comes From

Ikigai is a Japanese term that loosely translates to "a reason for being" — but the neat four-circle framework (what you love, what you're good at, what the world needs, what you can be paid for) isn't ancient philosophy taught in Japanese schools. It comes from a bestselling book that popularized the idea for a Western audience, framing it in a marketable, easy-to-diagram way. Ask someone in Japan about "ikigai" using this exact four-part model, and they likely won't recognize it.

That doesn't make the framework useless — it's being borrowed because it has real value as a thinking tool, not because it's an authentic ancient practice. Worth knowing, so you don't repeat the myth as fact.

## The Four Pillars

The four questions at the heart of ikigai: what you love (activities you'd do endlessly, regardless of skill), what you're good at (your actual skill set, whether natural or practiced), what the world needs (the problems that genuinely bother you — gaps in education, broken relationships, pollution, corruption), and what you can be paid for (skills that could realistically generate income).

Where these circles overlap, you get useful labels: love + skill is your **passion**. Skill + payment is your **profession**. Payment + what the world needs is your **vocation**. Love + what the world needs is your **mission**. The center, where all four meet, is theoretically your ikigai — where meaning, skill, contribution, and income line up.

## The Journal Questions

For each question below, aim for at least three answers — five if you can push yourself, since the fifth is often where real self-insight shows up.

### What You Love
1. What three to five things did you do as a kid (or still do) that make you completely lose track of time?
2. What three to five hobbies, from childhood to now, would people who know you immediately associate with you?
3. What three to five things have you experienced that you wish everyone would try (a food, a practice like meditation, a book)?
4. Who are three to five people you deeply admire — real or fictional, living or dead — and why specifically?

### What You're Good At
1. What three to five things do people consistently compliment you on?
2. What three to five things could you confidently handle if someone handed you the responsibility right now, no hesitation?
3. What three to five things do people repeatedly come to you for help with?
4. If a company had all your data — habits, interests, work — and generated job profiles for you regardless of what you enjoy, what roles would it likely suggest?
5. Given three to five years, unlimited money, and the best teachers in the world, which fields could you realistically reach an elite, even international, level in? Be honest about natural aptitude versus effort alone.

If these feel hard, ask someone who knows you well — a friend or family member often sees patterns we're too close to notice ourselves.

### What the World Needs
1. Who are three to five people, from any point in history, who you feel have done the most good for humanity, and why?
2. What are three to five of the biggest problems you believe the world faces right now? Note briefly why each ranks above the others.
3. If you had authority to introduce three to five new laws, what would they be — and why those over other options?
4. What are three to five of the biggest mistakes you think people repeatedly make in life?

### What You Can Be Paid For
1. What three to five fields do you notice where people are both making good money *and* seem genuinely happy?
2. What three to five skills do you already have, or could develop with more time and guidance, that could become real income?
3. What three to five things, if you spent three to five years learning them regardless of current enjoyment, would you bet on paying off financially?
4. What three to five professions have no real income ceiling — where more effort keeps translating into more reward?

## A Practical Note on Doing This Exercise

Don't rush it. If your thinking is already fairly clear, budget 45 minutes to an hour; if this is new territory, it could take closer to two hours, and that's normal. You can also split it across a few days rather than one sitting.

Once you've written your answers, consider feeding the full set of questions and answers into an AI tool and asking it to analyze the pattern. Having both question and answer gives it context to spot themes you might not consciously notice. Save that output somewhere you'll actually revisit — it's a reference point, not a one-time exercise.

## Takeaway and Next Steps

This is a foundation-setting exercise, not the finish line. The four categories aren't meant to be answered once and forgotten — they're raw material for the work ahead.

- Set aside 45 minutes to 2 hours, in one sitting or spread across a few days.
- Write at least three (ideally five) answers per question, across all four categories.
- Run your answers through an AI tool for a pattern analysis, and save the output.
- Move on to Part 2, where these questions connect to the practical goal-breakdown work in Part 3 — turning self-reflection into an actual plan.

This is Part 1 of a three-part series. Part 2 builds directly on what you uncover here, so it's worth doing the work now rather than skimming past it.
