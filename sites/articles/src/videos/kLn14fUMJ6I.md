# Why You're Always Tired

Everyone hits it eventually — that dragging, foggy exhaustion that shows up no matter how much you slept, or how little you actually did that day. In this video, Career from Living Foundations breaks down why we burn out on energy far too quickly, and walks through two big buckets of causes: the physical (diet, movement, sleep) and the psychological (how we use — and misuse — our own minds). The video focuses mostly on the second half, since diet and movement have already been covered in earlier videos on the channel.

Here's what actually drains you, and what to do about it.

## The Physical Layer: Diet and Movement

Before getting into the mental side, the video briefly names the physiological basics. If your diet is lacking — not enough protein, vitamins, minerals, or simply not enough calories — your body doesn't have the raw material to produce energy, and fatigue follows. Long-term deficiencies, like low magnesium, compound this over time.

The second physical driver is inactivity. A sedentary lifestyle — sitting most of the day, minimal movement — pushes your body's metabolism into a low-maintenance mode. Less movement gives your body less reason to actively supply energy, so production scales down to match. Moving more, not less, is often what raises your baseline energy back up. These topics are covered in more depth elsewhere on the channel; this video turns to something less obvious — how your mind's own habits quietly wear you down.

## The Mind Isn't Built for Constant Switching

The core psychological idea in this video is about focus, and what happens when you interrupt it.

When you sit down to concentrate — studying, working, any focused task — it takes your mind real time to warm up. The video estimates roughly 15–20 minutes before focus actually "locks in." Once it does, you get a solid stretch, maybe 40–50 minutes, of genuinely productive work before your mind naturally wants a short break.

The problem isn't that break itself. It's what people do with it. If, the moment focus dips, you reach for your phone, check social media, or jump into something highly stimulating, you don't just take a break — you short-circuit the whole warm-up process. Your mind was gently settling; a jolt of high-stimulation content resets it. When you try to go back to work, you're not resuming — you're starting the 15–20 minute warm-up all over again.

This is why some people who study or work for hours seem to get far less done than people who work in short, protected blocks. The student who studies in a quiet, undistracted stretch and *then* rests will get several times more out of that time than one who "studies," breaks into a video or scrolling session, and then has to re-earn focus each time. It's not really about hours spent — it's about how many times you force your mind to restart.

## Not All Rest Is Equal

A related point: the *kind* of break you take matters as much as the break itself. Highly stimulating, highly entertaining content — videos, scrolling, exciting conversations — leaves a kind of afterglow. Your mind stays keyed up even after you've stopped, and it takes real time to settle back down, delaying your return to focus.

Quieter, low-stimulation activities — sitting with a cup of tea, looking out a window, a short walk without your phone — let your mind ease down and back into work far more smoothly. A pull toward sleep or distraction mid-task is often your mind signaling it needs a small window of genuine, low-key rest, not a jolt of stimulation.

## Recovery, Addiction, and Mental Energy

The video also connects this pattern to addictive behavior more broadly — smoking, drinking, doom-scrolling, or compulsive phone use. These aren't just "bad habits" in isolation; they actively drain the same reserve of positive mental energy that focus and recovery depend on. The more your mind gets pulled into these spirals, the less energy it has left for everything else, including simply feeling rested.

This is offered as one real explanation for a common complaint: feeling tired despite not having "done" much. The exhaustion isn't always physical — it can be the residue of a mind that's been yanked back and forth between focus and high-stimulation distraction all day, never actually settling.

## Sleep, Screens, and Real Recovery

Sleep gets specific attention. The video pushes back on the idea that sleeping "too much" is inherently a problem — if your body is asking for eight, ten, even twelve hours, that's your body's own signal, and it deserves attention rather than guilt. If you're feeling depressed or unusually fatigued, permitting more sleep, not fighting it, is the more sensible response.

But sleep quality can be sabotaged the same way focus can. Scrolling on your phone in bed for hours before sleeping keeps your mind stimulated right up to the point you try to shut it off — and that carries into how you wake up. If you reach for your phone again first thing in the morning, you're skipping the natural, unhurried transition your mind needs, and undoing whatever recovery the night's sleep gave you.

## Takeaways

- **Get the basics right first.** Undereating, poor-quality food, and a sedentary lifestyle both directly lower your energy production — this groundwork matters before anything else.
- **Protect your warm-up.** Focus takes 15–20 minutes to build. Interrupting it with high-stimulation content forces you to restart from zero.
- **Choose calm breaks over stimulating ones.** Quiet, low-key rest lets your mind settle and return to work faster than scrolling or video-watching does.
- **Watch the addictive loop.** Smoking, drinking, and compulsive phone use quietly drain the same energy reserve your focus and mood depend on.
- **Don't fight your need for sleep.** If your body wants more rest, especially alongside low mood, let it — sleep debt and mental fatigue are connected.
- **Keep screens out of the transition zones.** Avoid heavy phone use right before bed and right after waking; both bookend your sleep and blunt its recovery value.
