# Why You Always Have a Headache

Headaches are one of those complaints almost everyone shrugs off — pop a pill, push through the day, forget about it until the next one arrives. But recurring headaches are rarely random. They tend to trace back to a small set of everyday causes: poor posture, dehydration, negative or overly stressed thinking, jaw tension, and a digestive system that isn't working the way it should. Once you know which of these is driving your headaches, you can actually do something about it — often without medication.

This piece is meant as an overview. Each of the causes below is a big enough topic to deserve its own deep dive, so think of this as a map: it tells you where to look, and which direction to dig further if a particular cause sounds like your situation.

## The Common Root Causes

Most everyday headaches come down to a handful of triggers:

- **Poor neck posture.** When the neck isn't aligned well — think hours of looking down at a phone or hunching over a desk — it can't properly support the head and upper back. That strain radiates upward into head pain.
- **Dehydration.** Simply not drinking enough water shows up as a headache far more often than people realize.
- **Negative or high-pressure thinking.** Persistent stress and negative thought patterns place the nervous system under strain, and that tension frequently manifests as head pain.
- **Digestive trouble.** Gas, bloating, and general digestive discomfort are also linked to headaches — when the gut is off, the effects aren't limited to the stomach.
- **Jaw tension.** Clenching or tightness in the jaw is another under-recognized contributor.

## Posture and the Neck Connection

If your posture has slipped — especially in the neck — your spine loses some of its ability to hold your head up properly. Over time, this shows up as tightness that pulls upward and creates headache pain, sometimes paired with upper back discomfort. The fix isn't a one-time stretch; it's rebuilding the habits (sitting, screen height, sleeping position) that keep your neck in a neutral position throughout the day.

## Pressure Points for Relief

For in-the-moment relief, the video walks through a few pressure points you can use with your own hands:

- **Base of the skull / upper neck.** Where the muscle along the back of the neck ends, just below the skull, press firmly on that point on both sides. A steady press here is one of the first things to try when a headache starts.
- **Temples.** Using circular, rubbing motion on the sides of the head near the temples can bring noticeable relief.
- **Between the eyebrows.** Gentle pressure and massage in this spot — sometimes called the "third eye" point in bodywork traditions — is another one worth trying.
- **Web of the hand.** On the back of the hand, in the fleshy area between the thumb and index finger, pressing about one to three finger-widths up from where the fingers meet can also ease tension.

None of these need special tools — just your fingers, applied with steady, deliberate pressure or slow circular motion.

## Jaw Tension and Relaxation

Tightness in the jaw — clenching, grinding, or simply holding tension there without noticing — is called out as its own contributing factor. Consciously relaxing the jaw, letting it hang loose rather than staying clenched, can reduce the tension that otherwise travels up into headache pain. This is worth paying attention to especially if you catch yourself clenching during stressful moments or while concentrating.

## Diet and Digestion

The video connects digestive health to headache frequency: when digestion is sluggish or gas builds up, that internal disturbance can translate into head pain. A few practical suggestions come up:

- Keep a simple, steady approach to diet rather than complicating it.
- Lemon is mentioned as something worth incorporating.
- A once-a-year full digestive reset or cleanse is suggested as a periodic practice.

The broader point is that nutrition and gut health deserve their own attention — they're not separate from headaches, they're often upstream of them.

## A Quick Remedy: Ginger

If nothing else, one simple remedy stands out: a small piece of fresh ginger. Ginger is described as helping clear and settle the stomach — useful if your gut feels heavy or unsettled. Chewing a small piece can bring relief relatively quickly, especially when digestive discomfort is part of what's driving the headache.

## Takeaways

- Recurring headaches usually trace back to one (or more) of five things: neck posture, dehydration, negative/stressful thinking, digestive trouble, or jaw tension.
- Check your neck posture, especially if you spend long hours at a screen or on your phone — poor alignment is a common hidden trigger.
- Try pressure-point relief: the base of the skull, the temples, the point between the eyebrows, and the web of the hand between thumb and index finger.
- Notice jaw tension and consciously relax it, particularly during stressful moments.
- Pay attention to your digestion — gas and bloating aren't just stomach issues, they can show up as head pain too.
- A small piece of fresh ginger can offer quick relief when digestive upset is part of the picture.
- This is general wellness information, not medical advice. If headaches are frequent, severe, or unusual for you, check with a qualified healthcare provider.
