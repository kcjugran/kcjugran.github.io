# 5 Static Holds to Do Before You Run (Better Form, Better Muscle Activation)

Most runners either skip warm-ups entirely or default to a few generic quad stretches before heading out the door. This video takes a different approach: five static holds, most drawn from Foundation Training principles, designed to wake up your posterior chain, teach your hips to move the way they should during a run, and give you a felt sense of alignment before you ever take a stride. None of these are complicated. What matters is the positioning — small details that change what muscles actually switch on.

Here's a breakdown of all five, and why they matter for your running form.

## 1. Sprinter Stretch (Sprinter Lunge)

Start in a lunge position, but instead of a casual lunge, line your feet up as if you were walking a slackline — one foot directly in front of the other, not staggered to the sides. Your front knee stays over your front heel. Your back knee lines up directly behind your back heel.

From here, hinge upward at the hips while keeping both legs straight and tight, both heels pressing into the ground, and a slight anterior tilt in your lower back — think of creating a gentle arch, not rounding forward. Because your feet are aligned front-to-back rather than side-to-side, your pelvis is forced to organize itself properly to hold the position. Arms stay straight overhead.

Hold this for roughly 60 to 90 seconds per side. You'll notice afterward that your walking and running gait feels a bit more aligned — like your hips and legs are tracking straighter under you.

## 2. Founder

This one comes straight from Foundation Training and is one of the more interesting hip-hinge patterns you can practice. Stand with your feet about double hip-width apart, toes pointed the same direction as your heels (a very slight internal rotation of the toes is fine, but keep it minimal).

Reach both arms forward, then send your hips backward while your arms reach further forward — essentially creating a tug-of-war between your hips moving back and your hands reaching out. Keep your weight shifted toward your heels. The more you bend your knees, the more you'll feel it in your glutes; the straighter you keep your legs, the more it shifts into your hamstrings.

This is a full posterior-chain activator — glutes, hamstrings, and the muscles running up your back — all switched on by one hinge pattern.

## 3. Wood Chopper (Split-Stance Founder)

Think of this as the Founder hinge, but performed from a lunge or "beast stance" instead of a wide parallel stance. Step into a lunge — one foot forward, one back, roughly the same distance you'd use for any lunge. Put about 80–90% of your weight into your front heel.

From there, run the same tug-of-war pattern: hips reach back, arms reach forward, maintaining a slight arch in your lower back with your chest lifted (anterior tilt). Bending the front knee more shifts the emphasis into the glute; straightening it shifts emphasis into the hamstring. Do one side, then switch legs.

Because this version is unilateral — one leg at a time — it also has a secondary benefit: it helps even out left-right imbalances that most runners carry without realizing it, since each side has to stabilize and activate independently.

## 4. Hip Lock

This move teaches you how to actually feel your glutes fire while running — specifically during hip extension, the moment your leg drives backward behind you mid-stride.

Stand tall, reach both arms straight overhead, then lift one knee up while the standing leg pushes down firmly through the ball of the foot. You're balancing on one leg while extending the standing hip and reaching upward through your arms — everything working in the same upward, locked-in line. That downward push through the grounded foot is what triggers the glute activation on that side.

The takeaway to carry into your run: when your leg extends behind you with each stride, that's the moment you should be actively engaging your hips — not letting it happen passively. This drill isolates and rehearses that exact movement pattern. Do one side, then switch.

## 5. Plank (a slightly different version)

Everyone knows planks, but the cueing here is more specific than the standard version. Start lying down, then lift your hips until your body is roughly parallel to the floor. From there, restore your spine's natural curvature: a slight anterior tilt, a light arch in your lower back, chest gently pointed forward and slightly exaggerated. You're not flattening your back — you're finding your normal spinal curve while holding the plank position.

Once that spinal position is set, straighten one knee, then the other. Holding this specific spinal alignment while both legs are extended makes it a notably more challenging plank than the standard version, and it's excellent for activating the entire front line of the body.

## Putting It Together

You can hold each of these anywhere from 30 seconds up to 90 seconds. Run through all five as a circuit and you're looking at roughly 6 to 9 minutes total — a short investment before a run.

Do this routine before you head out and pay attention to what changes: your stride tends to feel more controlled, your balance improves, and the run itself often feels noticeably easier to settle into. The goal isn't just flexibility — it's giving your hips, glutes, and hamstrings a quick rehearsal of the exact positions and extension patterns they'll need once you're actually moving.

## Quick Action Steps

- Do all five holds before your next run, in order: sprinter stretch, Founder, wood chopper (both sides), hip lock (both sides), plank.
- Hold each for 30–90 seconds; expect the full circuit to take about 6–9 minutes.
- Focus on the cues, not just the shape: heel-to-heel alignment in the sprinter stretch, the "tug-of-war" feel in Founder and wood chopper, active downward push in hip lock, and restoring your natural spinal arch in the plank.
- Notice the difference in your stride and balance once you start running — that felt sense is the real signal these are working.
