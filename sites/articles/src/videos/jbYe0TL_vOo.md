# Two Simple Ways to Clear a Blocked Nose

A stuffy nose can throw off your whole day — sleep, focus, even your mood. The good news is you don't need a spray or a pill to get some relief. This video walks through two simple, hands-on techniques you can use anytime your nose feels blocked: an acupressure-style massage, and a breath-hold exercise that gently trains your sinuses to open up. Here's a closer look at both, along with a few habits that support easier nasal breathing overall.

## Method One: Sinus Pressure Points

The first technique is a simple massage using pressure points around your face. Using your fingers, you press and trace a line starting from near the eyebrows, moving upward and inward, then following that same motion firmly down the side of the nose. The idea is a repeated pushing motion — from the inner brow area, along the side of the nose, and off toward the outer edge — done again and again.

Keep this up for about two to three minutes, pressing consistently rather than just lightly grazing the skin. Over that time, many people start to notice their sinuses beginning to loosen and open, which can bring some immediate relief from that heavy, blocked feeling.

This is the kind of technique you can do almost anywhere — at your desk, before bed, or the moment you notice congestion setting in — since it requires nothing but your hands.

## Method Two: The Breath-Hold Technique

The second method described in the video is presented as the more powerful of the two, and it's rooted in a breathing practice. It's worth noting the video mentions this is a simplified version of a more complete breathing practice, with a fuller explanation linked separately — what's shown here is a condensed, practical version.

Here's how it works:

1. Take two or three normal breaths in and out to settle yourself.
2. Take one more inhale, then pinch your nose closed and hold your breath with your lungs comfortably full (not straining).
3. Hold as long as you comfortably can. As you feel the urge to breathe build, you can nod your head gently or move slightly to help push through the last stretch.
4. When you really need to breathe, release and take a long, controlled breath back in through the nose.

After that first round, check in on how your nose feels — many people notice it's already a little more open than before. Then rest for thirty to forty seconds and repeat the cycle: a few normal breaths, one deeper inhale, hold, and release.

Do this daily, and the video suggests that your breath-hold time — and your ease of nasal breathing — tends to improve gradually with consistent practice.

## Why Nose Breathing Matters

Beyond the two techniques, the video makes a broader point worth sitting with: try to break the habit of mouth breathing whenever you can. Relying on your mouth to breathe, especially as a default habit, is linked to poorer overall breathing quality and, over time, can contribute to a shorter temper or general irritability. Whenever possible, aim to breathe through your nose instead.

Two practical suggestions are offered to help build this habit:

- **At night**, you can use tape over the mouth while sleeping to encourage nasal breathing through the night (a technique sometimes called mouth taping).
- **During the breathing exercise itself**, you can hold a small amount of water in your mouth. This acts as a simple built-in check — if you're tempted to breathe through your mouth, the water makes that obvious, keeping you honest about staying nasal.

## A Quick Note

This video shares general self-help techniques for nasal congestion and is not a substitute for medical advice. If you have chronic sinus issues, a deviated septum, allergies, or breathing difficulty that doesn't improve, it's worth checking in with a doctor rather than relying on these techniques alone.

## Practical Takeaways

- **Try the pressure-point massage first** for quick relief: trace from the inner eyebrow area down the side of the nose, pressing firmly, for two to three minutes.
- **Add the breath-hold cycle** when you want a deeper reset: a few normal breaths, one full inhale, hold until you feel the urge to breathe, then release slowly through the nose. Repeat after a thirty-to-forty-second rest.
- **Practice daily** — the breathing technique is described as cumulative, with nasal capacity improving gradually over time rather than instantly.
- **Build the nose-breathing habit** generally: consider mouth taping at night, and use the water-in-mouth trick during exercises to stay committed to nasal breathing.
