# 7 Fundamental Pranayama Techniques Everyone Can Practice

Pranayama is one of the oldest branches of yogic science — a system, thousands of years old, built around one idea: your breath is a lever you can pull to change your state. The mind wanders and feels hard to control directly, but by consciously moving air in and out in specific patterns, you can shift your physiology and, through it, your psychology. Some patterns calm you down. Others energize you. And beyond the physical effects, pranayama has always been tied to the subtler, spiritual side of yoga practice.

You've probably seen the popular image already: someone sitting cross-legged at dawn, doing rapid pumping breaths through the nose. That's usually just Kapalbhati — but pranayama is actually a family of techniques, each with a different job. Here are seven fundamental ones, what they do, and how to practice them.

## 1. Deep Breathing (The Warm-Up)

Start here. Sit comfortably and take slow, long breaths in and out through the nose — full inhales, full exhales, chest relaxed. You're maximizing the oxygen–carbon dioxide exchange and preparing the body for the more active techniques ahead. Do this for two to five minutes with a timer running. A little lightheadedness at first is normal.

## 2. Kapalbhati (Skull-Shining Breath)

This is the famous one — short, forceful exhales through the nose with passive inhales in between, done in quick succession. Sharply push air out by pulling your navel toward your spine, then let the inhale happen on its own; you don't need to think about breathing in. Keep your speed moderate — you don't need to go extremely fast for it to work. Start around 3–5 minutes and build toward 5–15 minutes over time, with a short rest between rounds if needed. It works the core, generates internal heat, and is considered excellent for digestion and energy.

## 3. Vayu Pranayama (with Agnisar Kriya)

This works the core and the energy centers around the chest and throat. Exhale fully, pull your belly button all the way in, and hold that emptied-out position — folding the torso slightly forward and, if your neck allows, tucking the chin toward the chest (jalandhara bandha, or throat lock). While holding the breath out, pump the emptied stomach in and out repeatedly — this pumping is Agnisar Kriya — three to five times per round, since it builds internal heat quickly.

## 4. Anulom-Vilom (Alternate Nostril Breathing)

Probably the best-known technique after Kapalbhati, and one of the simplest. Use your thumb and ring finger to alternately close one nostril: close the right, inhale through the left; close the left, exhale through the right; inhale through the right, close it, exhale through the left — continue alternating in a steady, unforced rhythm. The idea is balance: each nostril is linked to one side of your energetic system, and alternating brings the two into equilibrium. Build gradually toward matching your Kapalbhati time (many aim for around 15 minutes of each).

## 5. Bhramari (Humming Bee Breath)

A deeply relaxing practice. Close off your ears and eyes with your hands (thumbs on the ears, fingers resting over the eyes), breathe in, and hum a steady "mmm" as you exhale — feeling the vibration move through your head, throat, and chest. This stimulates the vagus nerve, which signals the body to relax. Use whatever pitch feels comfortable and doesn't strain your throat, and let the hum last as long as it naturally can without forcing it.

## 6. Om Chanting Pranayama

A close cousin of Bhramari. Take a full breath in and, as you exhale, chant a long "Om," letting the vibration travel through the body and stretching the exhale out as long as comfortable. Like Bhramari, this is calming rather than energizing.

## 7. Ujjayi-Style Throat Breath

The video also covers a technique using a gentle constriction at the back of the throat to create an audible, ocean-like sound on the breath. It's described as good for the throat and voice — practiced gently, letting the sound guide a slow, extended breath cycle rather than forcing it.

## Practical Guidance Before You Begin

A few ground rules the video is clear about:

- **Practice on an empty stomach.** Wait at least 2.5–3 hours after eating. Early morning is ideal; in the evening, leave at least 30 minutes before dinner.
- **Be cautious with the more vigorous techniques** (Kapalbhati, Agnisar Kriya) if you have high or low blood pressure, are pregnant, or are physically weak or currently unwell — better to avoid these and stick to the calming ones.
- **Posture matters less than consistency.** A tall spine is ideal, but sitting against a wall for support — or in a chair — is fine, especially for beginners. Build toward an unsupported seat over time as your body strengthens.
- **Start small and build up.** Begin with 10–15 minutes total. Around 15 minutes each of Kapalbhati and Anulom-Vilom is a reasonable target with practice; roughly 3–5 rounds is typical for Bhramari, Om chanting, and Agnisar Kriya.
- **Listen to your body over any fixed rule.** If a technique doesn't feel right yet, skip it and revisit it later.

## Takeaway: How to Actually Start

1. Pick a consistent time — ideally morning, on an empty stomach.
2. Warm up with a few minutes of slow deep breathing.
3. Add one energizing technique (Kapalbhati or Vayu Pranayama/Agnisar Kriya) if you're in good health, followed by Anulom-Vilom for balance.
4. Close with a calming technique — Bhramari or Om chanting — to settle the nervous system before you go about your day.
5. Start with short sessions (10–15 minutes total) and let the duration grow naturally with practice, rather than forcing intensity or length early on.

None of this needs to be complicated or intense to work. The consistency of showing up daily matters more than pushing hard in any single session.
