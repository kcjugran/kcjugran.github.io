# This Is How to Breathe Correctly

Breathing is the one thing we never stop doing and almost never think about. That's exactly the problem. Most of us breathe in a way that's shallow, tight, and disconnected from how our bodies were actually built to do it — and we don't notice, because breathing works well enough to keep us alive even when it's working poorly. This piece walks through what "correct" breathing actually looks like, why so many of us have drifted away from it, and a simple, practical way to relearn it.

## Breathing Is a Loop, Not Just an Exchange

At its most basic, breathing is an exchange: you pull in air carrying oxygen, your body uses it, and you push out the waste — carbon dioxide — that your cells no longer need. This exchange doesn't happen in isolation, either. What one living system releases becomes input for another, part of a larger cycle running well beyond our own lungs. Worth keeping in mind — breathing well isn't just a personal fitness metric, it's how we participate in that cycle properly.

## The Muscles Actually Doing the Work

Your lungs don't move air on their own — they're passive. The real engine sits below them: the diaphragm, a large dome-shaped muscle that separates your chest cavity from your abdomen. When you inhale, the diaphragm contracts and flattens downward, creating space and suction that pulls air in. When you exhale, it relaxes back upward, and air is pushed out.

There's a whole support cast around it too — muscles along the rib cage, the abdominal wall, and others that assist depending on how hard you're breathing. Broadly, think of it as two groups: muscles that help draw air in, and muscles that help push air out. When this system works as designed, your belly and ribs expand outward as you inhale and settle back down as you exhale — not your shoulders creeping up toward your ears.

## Why We Forget How to Breathe Properly

Here's the uncomfortable part: correct breathing isn't something most adults do by default anymore, and lifestyle is a big reason why. Poor eating habits and posture both play a role. When digestion is regularly upset — from rushed meals, poor food choices, or overeating — the irritation and tension around the stomach area affects the surrounding muscles, including the ones that support the diaphragm's movement. Add in a lack of real physical movement — not enough walking, running, or general activity — and those muscles lose their natural range and flexibility.

Over time, the body adapts to this reduced state as normal. It quietly forgets what full, correct breathing feels like and settles into a smaller, more restricted pattern. You don't decide to breathe shallowly — it happens gradually, as a side effect of how you live, eat, and move.

## What Shallow Breathing Actually Looks Like

A common giveaway: when you breathe in, do your shoulders rise? Does your chest puff up while your belly barely moves, or worse, pulls in? That's a sign of shallow, upper-chest breathing — a pattern that keeps the diaphragm from doing its full job. It might feel normal because it's familiar, but it's not the same as breathing that uses your full lung capacity and lets your core muscles do their part.

Correct breathing should let the belly expand outward on the inhale — not exaggerated, just naturally, the way it would if nothing were restricting it.

## The 360-Degree Breath

One useful way to think about full breathing is that it shouldn't just move forward — it should expand all the way around: front, sides, and back. Picture your ribcage and lower torso as a cylinder expanding in every direction as you inhale, then relaxing fully as you exhale. This is sometimes called 360-degree breathing, and it's a good check for whether you're using your whole diaphragm and rib structure, not just the front of your belly.

A simple way to build this awareness: wrap a towel (or similar band) snugly around your lower ribs, roughly just below your chest and above your navel. As you inhale, try to push outward against the towel — front, sides, and back. The resistance gives you feedback on exactly where you're expanding and where you're not. As you exhale, relax fully and let the ribs and belly settle back down.

Keep the effort light. This isn't about forcing a huge breath — a small, controlled expansion against the towel is enough to start training the pattern. Over time this doubles as a gentle stretch through the ribs and lower back, and builds general body awareness, not just breathing mechanics.

## A Word of Caution

One method won't be identical for everyone. Different people carry tension differently, and a pattern that feels natural for one person may take longer to settle in for another. If your form feels off — breathing too low or too shallow relative to where you're focusing — ease back and slow down rather than pushing intensity. And this isn't meant to be forced into every waking moment; treat it as focused practice sessions, not a nonstop effort.

## Takeaway: How to Practice This

- Check your default pattern first. Breathe normally and notice: do your shoulders rise, or does your belly and ribcage expand?
- Use a towel or band around your lower ribs as a feedback tool. Inhale and try to push outward — front, sides, and back — against it.
- Keep the effort gentle. This is about awareness and range, not forcing a maximal breath.
- Practice in short, focused sessions rather than trying to consciously control every breath all day.
- Be patient with posture and digestion too — since both feed into how freely your breathing muscles can move, cleaning those up supports the breathing work, not just the other way around.
- Try it for yourself and notice what changes over a few sessions. Everyone's breathing pattern is a little different, so pay attention to what actually works for your body rather than forcing someone else's exact experience.
