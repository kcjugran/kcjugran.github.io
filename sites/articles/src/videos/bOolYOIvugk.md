# The Perfect Plank: A Step-by-Step Guide to Getting Your Form Right

Walk into any gym and you'll see plenty of planks — and just as many compensations. Someone's back is sagging, someone else's lower back is aching, a third person's shoulders are creeping up around their ears. If you've ever felt like your plank "shouldn't" hurt where it hurts, you're not imagining it. Something in the setup is usually off.

This companion piece walks through the approach from the video: instead of throwing a list of cues at you, it builds the plank from the ground up by first teaching your body two specific movement skills, then combining them into the position itself. It's more detailed than most plank tutorials — and that's the point. Once you understand *why* each cue matters, holding a proper plank stops being a guessing game.

## Why Most Plank Cues Don't Stick

The usual advice — "keep your back straight," "brace your core" — assumes you already have control over your spine's position. Most people don't, not consciously. Before you can hold a plank correctly, you need to know what it feels like to move your lower back independently of your upper back, and your upper back independently of your lower back. That's the real skill being taught here, and it starts on the ground, not in the plank itself.

## Step 1: Learn to Move Your Lower Back (Hips) Without Moving Your Upper Body

Start in a crawling position — hands under shoulders, knees under hips, side-on so you can see the movement.

First, get familiar with spinal flexion and extension: round your back up toward the ceiling, then let it sag and look up toward the sky. A couple of reps so your body understands what "up" and "down" through the spine feels like.

Now the actual skill: lock your head and upper back — fix your eyes on a spot on the floor so your neck and thoracic spine stop moving. With the upper body locked, move only from your lower back and hips: a posterior pelvic tilt (tucking the pelvis under, rounding the lower back), then an anterior pelvic tilt (tipping the pelvis forward, arching the lower back) — chest and head staying still throughout.

This is the single most important piece in the whole tutorial. Learning to tilt the pelvis without dragging your upper body along is what separates a controlled plank from a collapsing one.

## Step 2: Learn to Move Your Upper Back Without Moving Your Lower Back

Now the reverse. Round and lock your lower back in place, and this time move only your chest and upper back: point your chest down toward your knees (rounding), then point it forward and up (extending), keeping your head roughly fixed and your lower back locked the entire time.

Between Step 1 and Step 2, you've now isolated the two movements you'll combine into the plank itself: hips moving while the upper body stays still, and upper back moving while the lower back stays still.

## Step 3: The Knee Plank

Drop down onto your elbows and knees, forming your first plank variation. Walk your knees back so your body is roughly parallel to the floor — not too low.

Here's where the two skills come together: a small, controlled arch in the lower back (not a sag, not a flat tuck), combined with the chest pointing slightly forward and up, using the upper-back movement from Step 2, so it stays flat rather than rounded or collapsed.

That combination — chest facing mostly forward and flat, lower back holding a small arch — is what creates real tension in the abs. Too much arch and the lower back takes over; too much rounding and you lose the effect. The sweet spot is subtle, and you'll feel it directly in your abs, not your lower back, when it's right. Hold this variation for roughly 90 seconds before progressing.

## Step 4: The Full Plank

From the knee plank position, walk your knees back and straighten your legs — this is the actual full plank. Set up the same way: hips roughly level so your upper arms are close to perpendicular to the floor, a small arch in the lower back, chest pointed slightly forward rather than rounded.

Done properly, this looks similar to a standard plank from across the room — but the execution makes it dramatically harder. Expect it to feel far more intense in your core within seconds, even if you've held planks for a long time before.

## How to Know You're Doing It Right

Use these sensations as your checklist:

- **Lower abs, around the navel** — this is where the primary effort should show up.
- **Shoulders** — some pressure here is normal since your arms are supporting you.
- **Upper or mid-back** — a stretching or working sensation here, combined with strong ab engagement, is actually a good sign that you're executing the variation correctly.
- **Lower back pain or strain** — this is your signal something's off. It usually means you've let the arch get too big, or your hips have dropped too low, compressing the lower back. Reduce the arch and lift the hips slightly.

## Practical Takeaways

- Before you plank, drill the two isolation movements on your hands and knees: (1) tilt your pelvis forward and back while keeping your upper back still, (2) round and extend your upper back while keeping your lower back still.
- In the plank itself, aim for a small, controlled arch in the lower back — not flat, not sagging.
- Point your chest slightly forward and up rather than letting it round or collapse.
- Check in with where you feel it: lower abs and possibly mid-back mean you're on track; sharp lower-back discomfort means dial back the arch or lift your hips.
- Build from the knee plank (roughly 90 seconds) before moving to the full plank.
- If the cues feel confusing on a first read, that's normal — this is a detail-heavy skill. Watching the demonstration more than once and practicing the two ground drills first will make it click faster than reading alone.
