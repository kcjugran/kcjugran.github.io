# Celibacy: A Common-Sense Look at an Uncomfortable Topic

Celibacy, or *brahmacharya*, is one of those subjects that carries a lot of weight in Indian spiritual culture — treated as sacred, but also surrounded by discomfort and avoidance. Before getting into what it actually means, it helps to acknowledge the awkwardness honestly rather than pretend it isn't there. Mastering your emotions and impulses is, within yogic tradition, considered one of the highest values. There's nothing wrong with that pursuit itself. The problem starts with how the word "celibacy" gets used.

## When "Celibacy" Becomes an Excuse

A recurring pattern is worth naming plainly: people sometimes use the language of celibacy to hide from something else, or to manufacture guilt where none needs to exist. In particular, there's a common trend among men who lack the social skills or confidence to relate to women — declaring "brahmacharya" becomes a convenient excuse to avoid that discomfort altogether. But sitting across from someone and still viewing them primarily through a physical lens, still projecting thoughts onto their body, isn't celibacy of the mind — at best it's celibacy of the body only. And that distinction matters, because a mind that is truly at peace doesn't need to force the body into restriction. If the mind is settled, the body follows without struggle.

## The Real Marker of Celibacy

A useful test: someone who is genuinely celibate, in the fullest sense, could have the opportunity for sex every day and it wouldn't shift anything in them — not because they're suppressing desire, but because sex simply doesn't have ownership over their attention. No particular person or moment provokes it. Their internal state stays calm regardless, and nothing essential in their life is missing as a result. That's the marker of the real thing, not the forced version.

## Semen Retention and the Energy Myth

The video also pushes back on a popular claim: that retaining semen alone produces some special rise in "sexual energy," visible as a certain glow or vitality in the face. Comparing this to men who undergo a vasectomy — who also report feeling tired after sex despite no ejaculatory fluid loss — the point made is that the fatigue or vitality people associate with semen retention has less to do with the act of retention itself and more to do with cultivating overall stability in oneself. In other words, don't chase a shortcut to that "glow." It comes from internal balance, not from a bodily mechanism alone.

## Redirecting Energy Toward Purpose

Rather than framing restraint as the goal, the emphasis shifts to expanding self-understanding — understanding of yourself, of women, and of your own purpose. That means clearing out the time-wasting, distracting elements of life and moving instead toward the things you're genuinely passionate about. Within a real relationship, sex is described not as a distraction but as a way of connecting with a partner — simply another way of expressing love toward another person. If that concept feels foreign or has "gone over your head," it may just mean you haven't been in a long-term relationship — which is presented as perfectly fine, not a flaw.

## The Lion Behind the Chain

One of the more vivid images offered: if you never engage with sex or intimacy at all, nothing about you fundamentally changes just by abstaining — the idea that a person will spontaneously develop some divine celibate power through sheer denial is called a kind of self-deception. Restraint through force is compared to keeping a lion locked behind a chain. The day that chain breaks, the lion doesn't emerge calm — it comes out ready to attack. But a lion that has been allowed to roam, relax, and feel at ease loses the urgent need to lash out; it becomes naturally still. The suggestion is that this stillness can't be achieved by physically restraining desire — it comes through meditation and through consciously setting your life's priorities. The creative force involved, it's noted, is simply too powerful to be controlled by will alone.

## Solitude, Relationships, and a Warning

The video touches on the value of solitude — spending time alone, contemplating one's own mortality, and asking what you want to do with your life before it ends — as a genuine practice worth taking seriously if you actually want to understand celibacy. At the same time, it cautions against retreating from the world entirely: if you're always running to the mountains, what happens to the world you left behind? Relationships and people, it's argued, serve as teachers — they reflect back what is good in you while also showing you where you still need to grow. Every relationship carries both joy and a lesson.

Finally, there's a direct and serious caution aimed particularly at those following spiritual or yogic paths: physical celibacy is a fine personal choice, but a significant number of cases of sexual misconduct trace back to spiritual settings and figures. Followers are urged to think carefully about this pattern, and — as directly stated — to stop lying to themselves about their own motives and restraint.

## Takeaways

- Genuine celibacy is a state of mind, not just physical abstinence — restricting the body while the mind stays fixated isn't the same thing.
- Using "celibacy" to avoid discomfort (such as social anxiety around relationships) is a trap worth recognizing in yourself.
- The idea that semen retention alone creates special energy or a visible "glow" is challenged — stability comes from balance, not restriction.
- Sexuality within a relationship is framed as one way of expressing love and connection, not inherently a distraction.
- Forced suppression of desire doesn't dissolve it — it tends to resurface, sometimes forcefully; real change comes through meditation and clear priorities, not willpower alone.
- Solitude and reflection on mortality have value, but so does staying engaged with people and relationships, which act as teachers.
- The talk closes with a pointed warning: much sexual misconduct originates in spiritual spaces, and self-honesty about one's own motives is essential.
