# Relationship Zen: A Simple Way to Decide Who to Keep and Who to Let Go

Most of us overcomplicate relationships. We agonize over whether to pursue a friendship, whether to keep trying with someone, whether to feel guilty about pulling away from another. The truth is, once you strip away the noise, there are really only four kinds of relationships you'll ever have with anyone — and once you can name which one you're in, the decision about what to do next becomes much easier.

## The Four-Box Framework

Picture a simple grid. On one side: do you like this person? On the other: do they like you? Every relationship in your life falls into one of four combinations:

1. **You like them, and they like you.**
2. **You like them, but they don't like you.**
3. **They like you, but you don't like them.**
4. **Neither of you likes the other.**

That's it. Nearly every confusing, emotionally draining situation — "should I keep trying with this person," "why do I feel guilty about this friendship," "why does this keep bothering me" — can be sorted into one of these four boxes. Once it's sorted, the right response usually becomes obvious.

## When You Like Someone, But They Don't Like You Back

This is the situation that causes the most pain, and the most bad behavior. The video points to a common and telling example: someone gets turned down or rejected by a person they liked, and instead of accepting it, they become bitter, angry, even obsessive — following the person around, resenting them, unable to let go.

The core teaching here is simple but hard to actually live by: **it is someone's right to not want to be with you.** Not liking someone back isn't a crime, an insult, or something that needs to be argued against. It's a choice, and it deserves respect — even when it stings. Chasing someone after they've made their answer clear, or turning cold and resentful because they didn't choose you, only adds suffering on top of an already disappointing moment. The healthier path is to acknowledge the rejection, respect their decision, and direct your energy elsewhere.

## When Someone Likes You, But You Don't Like Them Back

This box gets less airtime, but it's just as important — and it's the one where guilt tends to creep in. Someone enjoys your company, admires you, wants to talk to you or be closer to you, and you simply don't feel the same pull. Maybe you don't want to keep talking to them the way they want to keep talking to you.

Here the advice mirrors the first box, just flipped: **you don't owe someone your time or affection just because they offer theirs.** You can respect that someone likes you — their feelings are valid and don't need to be dismissed or mocked — while still not obligating yourself to reciprocate. Giving them honest space, rather than dragging things out to avoid an awkward moment, is the more respectful move for both of you. Politely declining further closeness ("thank you, but I don't want to move forward this way") is not cruelty; it's clarity.

## When Neither of You Likes the Other

This one is the easiest to navigate, even if it feels a little strange in the moment. You don't enjoy their company, and they don't enjoy yours. There's no real loss here — just two people who don't naturally connect. The instinct might be to overanalyze it or feel bad about the disconnect, but there's nothing to fix. You simply give each other space and move on without friction.

## Where to Actually Invest: Mutual Liking

The whole point of sorting relationships this way is to reveal where your energy actually belongs. The video's clearest instruction is this: **spend most of your time with the people who are in box one — the ones who like you, and whom you like back.** These are the relationships that don't require performance. You don't have to put on an act, force a version of yourself, or manage the interaction carefully. Being around them is easy, and that ease is the whole reward.

By contrast, trying to force closeness with people who don't want it, or maintaining relationships that drain you because you feel obligated, pulls energy away from the connections that are already working — the ones where the "chemistry," as the video calls it, exists naturally in both directions.

## Takeaways

- **Sort the relationship first, then decide how to act.** Ask: do I like them, and do they like me? The answer tells you what kind of response actually fits the situation.
- **If someone doesn't like you back, let them go with respect.** Their choice isn't an insult — chasing or resenting them only hurts you more.
- **If you don't like someone back, be honest and give space rather than dragging it out.** You can respect their feelings without matching them.
- **Mutual dislike needs no drama.** Just step back — there's nothing to repair.
- **Put your real time into mutual-liking relationships.** Those are the ones that don't need you to perform, and they're worth protecting.
