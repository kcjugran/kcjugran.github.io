# Finding Your Ikigai: A Practical Way to Figure Out What You Actually Want to Do

If you've ever sat with the question "what do I actually want to do with my life?" and come up blank, you're not alone. Most people fall into one of two camps: either they have no clue what they want, or they're simply confused, weighed down by family expectations, financial responsibility, and the quiet feeling that they're going through the motions without knowing why. Both are exhausting places to be stuck.

The Japanese concept of Ikigai offers a structured way out of that fog. The word itself roughly translates to "reason for being" — the thing that makes getting up in the morning worthwhile. It's not a mystical idea; it's a simple framework built around four overlapping questions about your life.

## The Four Circles

Ikigai is usually drawn as four overlapping circles, each representing a different lens on your life:

- **What you love** — the things you genuinely enjoy, that you'd do even without a reason.
- **What you're good at** — the skills and abilities other people notice and respect in you.
- **What you can be paid for** — the things the market will actually pay you to do.
- **What the world needs** — the problems or gaps you sense out there, in your community or in the world at large.

Where these circles overlap, you get something more specific. Love plus being good at something gives you **passion** — work that feels good and comes naturally, even if it doesn't pay well or change the world. Being good at something plus getting paid for it gives you **profession** — competent, well-compensated work that may not excite you or serve any larger purpose. Being paid plus the world needing it gives you a kind of **vocation** — meaningful in its impact, but not necessarily something you love or excel at. And loving something the world needs, without pay or mastery yet, gives you a **mission**.

Ikigai sits in the center, where all four overlap: something you love, that you're good at, that the world needs, and that you can be paid for. That's the target — not because every job needs to hit all four, but because naming which quadrants you're missing tells you exactly what to work on.

## Why This Actually Helps

The value of this model isn't the diagram — it's that it breaks an overwhelming question ("what should I do with my life?") into four much smaller, answerable ones. Instead of trying to solve everything at once, you interrogate each circle separately, and slowly a pattern of overlap starts to emerge.

## Questions to Sit With for Each Circle

**What you love.** Start a simple list. What do you do that makes time disappear — hiking, photography, stargazing, long conversations with friends, gaming, sitting in nature? Don't filter yourself; write down everything, even things that feel small or silly. A useful trick here: ask your parents or an older relative what you used to do constantly as a kid, around age seven or eight, before you had any sense of what you were "supposed" to like. That unfiltered instinct is often a real clue. Another angle — think about a moment in a film or story that moved you enough to imagine yourself doing it (wanting to perform on stage after watching a musician in a movie, for instance). That flicker of "I want to do that" is worth writing down too.

**What you're good at.** Think about moments when people were visibly impressed by something you did — a skill, a way you handled a difficult conversation, a negotiation, a piece of work that made someone say "that's really well done." These reactions from other people are often more reliable evidence of your strengths than your own self-assessment, because we tend to underrate the things that come easily to us.

**What you can be paid for.** This is more literal: what skills do you have, or could you build, that the market already pays for? Photography, writing, proofreading, teaching, consulting — anything where there's a clear demand and a track record of people paying for that kind of work.

**What the world needs.** This one asks you to look outward. What feels broken or missing to you — in food systems, agriculture, education, healthcare, environmental sustainability? You don't need a fully formed answer; just notice what keeps pulling at your attention when you read the news or talk to people.

## The Exercise

For each of the four circles, write down at least 20 ideas — and if you're serious about this, push to 50. The first ten or fifteen ideas you write will usually be obvious and average. It's the ideas that come after that — when you're forcing yourself past the easy answers — that tend to be the real insights. Don't filter as you go; write whatever comes, even if some of it sounds unremarkable. The volume matters because it exhausts your default answers and forces something more honest to surface.

Do this thinking alongside people who know you well. Talk it through with a close friend or relative, or take it on a long walk by yourself. Both tend to loosen up insight in ways that sitting and staring at a blank page doesn't.

## Takeaways

- Ikigai breaks down into four questions: what you love, what you're good at, what you can be paid for, and what the world needs.
- The overlaps between these circles have names — passion, profession, vocation, mission — and naming your gap tells you what to work on next.
- For each circle, write down at least 20 ideas, ideally 50; the later, harder-won ideas are usually more revealing than the first ones.
- Asking people who knew you as a child, and noticing when others are impressed by you, are both useful shortcuts to seeing yourself more clearly.
- This is a starting exercise, not a final answer — treat it as a way to generate raw material you can keep refining.
