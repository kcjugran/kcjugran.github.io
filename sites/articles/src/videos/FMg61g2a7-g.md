# Thinking About Quitting Your Soul-Crushing Job? Read This First

Almost everyone who talks to me about career and work ends up circling the same complaint: "I hate my job." Office politics, a promotion that never comes, a manager who plays favorites, a role that feels pointless day after day. It's one of the most common things people bring up, and it's rarely simple. This isn't a pep talk telling you to storm out on Monday. It's a slower, more honest look at what's actually going on when a job feels unbearable — and what to do about it before you make a big move.

## The Complaint Behind the Complaint

A lot of job frustration isn't really about the job itself. It's office politics. It's not knowing what you actually want. It's the gap between "I need the paycheck" and "I have no idea what I'd rather be doing instead." People say things like "I don't know what I like" or "at least the money's coming in" — and then stay stuck, unhappy, but unwilling to name what would actually make them happier. That confusion is the real starting point, not the job title on your resume.

## Be Honest About What "Practical" Really Means

There's a familiar script that runs in a lot of people's heads: figure out your passion, but only if it also pays well and helps the world — and if that takes too long to sort out, forget it, go find a stable job instead. Five days of work, weekends free, promotions on schedule, salary bump every so often. Nothing wrong with wanting stability. The problem is calling the passion route "impractical" without ever actually testing it. Spending a few weeks doing real research into what you enjoy isn't impractical — abandoning your one working life to a job you resent, without ever checking the alternative, arguably is the less practical choice.

## How Most People Actually Choose a Career (And Why It's Flawed)

Here's the pattern: you pick a stream before college even starts, based on what your parents suggest, what marks you got, what sounds respectable. You get into a course, you finish it, grades come out "okay," and then you slide into whatever job matches that degree. Years pass. If you're in government, promotions happen on a timeline regardless. If you're in the private sector, you work harder, but probably not as brutally as people assume. Either way, the job becomes furniture in your life — not chosen so much as inherited from a decision an 18-year-old version of you made with almost no real information.

## Who Are You Actually Taking Advice From?

This is the part worth sitting with. A career advisor tells an 18-year-old which course to pick, which college to choose — advice based on the advisor's own maturity and experience at that moment. Ten or fifteen years later, is that same advisor still handing out career advice to today's 18-year-olds from that same old vantage point, or have they actually lived and adjusted since? The video pushes back hard on taking career direction from people — including well-meaning family or "successful" figures — who aren't actually equipped to know you. Nobody has some magic inner voice that instantly reveals your purpose. The real answer is a process: you sit down, you do the work yourself.

## The Actual Process

Instead of waiting for clarity to strike, or outsourcing the decision to whoever sounds confident, the suggestion is straightforward:

- Talk to people — a lot of people, in different fields.
- Do your own research rather than accepting secondhand opinions as fact.
- Get comfortable being "impractical" for a few weeks while you explore, rather than committing to a lifetime of work you dislike.
- Expect this to take real time — days, maybe weeks — not a single afternoon of soul-searching.

Constantly jumping between careers or chasing every new idea isn't the goal either. The point is doing enough deliberate exploration once, properly, so you're not sleepwalking into a life you didn't choose.

## Clarity Is a Mindset Problem Too

A recurring idea here is that confusion about your career is often a symptom of a mind that hasn't been given clear inputs — not a permanent personality trait. If you feel foggy about what you want, that's often just unprocessed thinking, not proof that you're someone who'll never know. Power, in the sense of having real choices and control over your life, comes from doing this work. Without it, you're reacting to whatever job or advice lands in front of you instead of steering deliberately.

## It's Not Just About You

There's a final, grounding note in the message: getting clear on your work and building real capability isn't purely selfish. The more capable and secure you become, the more you're able to actually take care of the people around you. A life spent unhappy in the wrong job doesn't just cost you — it limits what you can offer everyone who depends on you.

## Takeaways

- Hating your job is often really about confusion, not the job description itself. Name what's actually bothering you.
- Don't dismiss the "passion" path as impractical without a real, time-boxed attempt at figuring it out first.
- Question the career script you inherited — degree, job, promotion — especially if you never actually chose it.
- Be skeptical of career advice from people who haven't kept growing since they gave it, including advice given to your younger self.
- Nobody has a shortcut to instant self-knowledge. Clarity comes from a deliberate process: talk to people, research, reflect, test.
- Give yourself weeks, not minutes, to explore — but don't turn exploration into permanent career-hopping.
- Getting your own life sorted isn't selfish — it's what lets you actually show up for the people who rely on you.
