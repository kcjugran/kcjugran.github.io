# Five Quick Moves to Ease Neck and Shoulder Pain

Neck and shoulder tightness is one of the most common complaints people bring to a movement coach, and it's easy to see why. Most of us spend hours a day hunched forward — over phones, laptops, steering wheels — and that posture slowly pulls the shoulders up and forward, tightens the chest, and leaves the neck doing work it was never meant to do. The good news is that you don't need an hour-long routine to feel relief. A short, focused sequence of five exercises can loosen things up quickly and, done regularly, start to retrain better posture over time.

This is general information, not medical advice. If you have significant pain, numbness, or a history of shoulder or neck injury, check with a physical therapist or doctor before starting any new exercise.

## Start With the Egyptian

The first move looks a little unusual the first time you see it, but it's simple once you understand the pattern. Keep your elbows at the same height as your shoulders, and rotate your arms — one hand turning to face up, the other down — while your head turns to follow whichever hand is rotating back. The part people miss is the shoulders: they should stay down the entire time. If your shoulders creep up toward your ears, the exercise loses most of its benefit. The whole point is training your shoulder blades to sit low and stable while your arms and neck move independently of them.

## A Close Cousin: Arm Raises With Rotation

The second exercise builds directly on the first. Raise your arms, turn your head to look toward the same side, and rotate. It's very similar to the Egyptian in feel and intent — again, the shoulders are the thing to watch. Keep them relaxed and down rather than letting them ride up with the arm movement.

## Simple Arm Rotations — With a Catch

The third exercise is a form you'll recognize from yoga: simple arm rotations. It looks easy, but done correctly it isn't especially comfortable, and that's by design. As you bring your elbows forward, actively think about pulling them fully together in front of you. As you swing them back, think about opening your chest as wide as it will go — really imagining your elbows being pulled apart. That combination of a full forward squeeze and a full backward opening is what gives this version of the movement its stretch, and with consistent practice it noticeably improves shoulder mobility.

## Scapula Depression, Protraction, and Retraction

The fourth movement targets shoulder strength and posture directly. Keep your arms straight with no bend at the elbows, and move through scapula depression, protraction, and retraction — in plain terms, drawing your shoulder blades down, then forward, then squeezing them back together. Because the arms stay straight, the work falls where it should: on the muscles that control the shoulder blade itself, which are exactly the muscles that tend to weaken with a lot of sitting and screen time.

## Finish With a Chest Opener

The last exercise is a dynamic chest stretch, and it's particularly useful because so many people carry tightness across the chest without realizing it — a natural result of living in a forward-leaning, screen-facing posture. Inhale as you open your arms out to about a 45-degree angle, opening your mouth at the same time so the stretch reaches through the chest and jaw together. Then exhale as you round forward slightly, letting the whole area relax. This move opens the chest and neck muscles together, and for anyone who holds tension in that region, it tends to bring noticeable relief through the shoulders as well.

## Takeaways

- The Egyptian and the arm-raise rotation both depend on keeping your shoulders down — let them rise and you lose the benefit.
- Simple arm rotations work best when you exaggerate the range: fully squeeze the elbows together going forward, fully open the chest going back.
- Scapula depression, protraction, and retraction — done with straight arms — build the strength and posture support that keeps shoulder pain from coming back.
- A dynamic chest stretch, paired with breath, releases tension that builds up from constant forward-leaning posture.
- None of these movements require equipment or much time — just attention to form, especially keeping the shoulders relaxed throughout.
