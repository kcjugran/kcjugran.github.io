# What Is Calisthenics? A Simple Starting Point

If you've ever wondered whether you need a gym membership and a rack of weights to get strong, calisthenics offers a reassuring answer: not necessarily. At its core, calisthenics means training with your own body weight as resistance, rather than external loads like barbells or dumbbells. No fancy equipment, no machines — just you, gravity, and a handful of foundational movement patterns.

This is meant to be the first lesson in a short series, laying the groundwork before moving into how to actually build a training program around these ideas.

## The Five Pillars of Calisthenics

Rather than treating bodyweight training as a random grab-bag of exercises, it helps to organize it around five core movement categories. According to the video, these are considered the most important elements of calisthenics:

1. **Pushing exercises**
2. **Pulling exercises**
3. **Squatting exercises**
4. **Bridging exercises**
5. **Ab (core) exercises**

Each category targets a different movement pattern, and together they cover most of what the body is capable of doing. Let's look at each one a little closer.

### Pushing

Pushing exercises are about moving weight away from your body. The classic example here is the push-up, but the category also includes dips and variations like bear crawling — movements that challenge the chest, shoulders, and triceps while asking your core to stay stable throughout.

### Pulling

Where pushing moves things away, pulling brings them toward you. Pull-ups are the most familiar version, but for those not yet ready for a full pull-up, Australian pull-ups (performed at an incline, with feet on the ground) are a great regression. Towel rows are mentioned as another accessible pulling variation — useful if you don't have access to a pull-up bar but still want to train that same pattern.

### Squatting

The squat pattern covers any movement where you bend at the hips and knees to lower your body — think squats, lunges, and their many jumping variations. This is fundamental not just for building leg strength, but for everyday movements like sitting, standing, and climbing stairs.

### Bridging

Bridging is described as an often-overlooked but important category. It includes movements like chakrasana (the wheel pose, or back bridge), hip bridges, and dhanurasana (bow pose) — all of which stretch and strengthen the muscles along the back of the body. This category is particularly valuable for counteracting the forward-hunched posture so many of us develop from sitting and screen time.

### Abdominals

Interestingly, the video notes that ab-specific training isn't strictly 100% necessary if you're already doing the other four categories well — many of them engage the core indirectly. Still, there's a personal belief expressed here that abdominals are worth training separately and deliberately, rather than relying only on incidental core engagement from other movements.

## Why Think in Categories, Not Just Exercises

The real value of this framework isn't the specific exercises themselves — it's the mental model. Instead of memorizing a list of moves, you start recognizing that any new exercise you come across probably fits into one of these five patterns. That makes it much easier to build a balanced routine, swap in variations as you progress, and notice if you're neglecting an entire category (a common mistake — many beginners overdo pushing and squatting while ignoring pulling and bridging).

## Takeaways

- Calisthenics is bodyweight training — using your own body as resistance instead of external weights or machines.
- There are five foundational movement categories: pushing, pulling, squatting, bridging, and core work.
- Pushing includes push-ups and dips; pulling includes pull-ups, Australian pull-ups, and towel rows; squatting includes squats, lunges, and jump variations; bridging includes chakrasana, hip bridges, and dhanurasana.
- Ab training isn't strictly required if the other four categories are trained well, but it's still worth including deliberately.
- This is the first part of a multi-part series — the next step is turning these categories into an actual training program.
