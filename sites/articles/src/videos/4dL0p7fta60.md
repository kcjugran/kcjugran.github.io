# Find Your Purpose, Part 3: Turning Clarity Into a Plan You Can Actually Run

If you've worked through Part 1 (the Ikigai questions) and Part 2 (the five daily journaling prompts), you've done the hard inner work. You have a rough sense of what you love, what you're good at, what the world needs from you, and where your life has been pulling you. That's clarity. But clarity alone doesn't move you anywhere — which is the question this part answers: you know what you want, so now what do you actually do?

This builds directly on the first two parts. If you're starting here, the exercises will still make sense, but your goals will land better once you've done that earlier groundwork.

## Where Your Life Direction Actually Comes From

Before planning, it's worth naming where purpose tends to come from. There are really only two sources.

The first is natural interest — something you gravitated toward before anyone told you to: drawing as a toddler with no encouragement, or endlessly happy playing sports as a kid. Nobody assigned it; you just leaned toward it.

The second, often more powerful, is what gets called a "void" — a gap or pain point from your own life. Maybe you were physically weak as a child, and that lack pushed you into serious fitness later. Maybe you grew up without money, and that absence now fuels your drive for financial security. The classic example is entrepreneurs who build entire missions — feeding people, funding education — around a childhood wound rather than a lifelong hobby. Purpose isn't always born from loving a thing; sometimes it's born from its absence.

Both are valid engines. Notice which one (or both) is driving what you wrote down in Parts 1 and 2.

## The Four Categories Your Goals Live In

Whatever you want out of life, it almost always sorts into four buckets:

- **Health** — physical and mental.
- **Relationships** — close, private ones and wider public/social ones.
- **Career** — work that's genuinely your passion, and work you do because it serves others or pays the bills.
- **Finance** — active income (what you earn working) and passive income (investments and anything that earns without daily effort).

That's eight areas total that account for most of what people chase in life. Take a moment now and write down: if everything went your way, where would you want to be in each category a year from now? Six-pack or lower stress? Out of a toxic relationship or into a good one? A raise, a career change, a promotion? Better investing knowledge or a side income? You need your own answer here, not someone else's.

## Why the One-Year Goal Isn't Enough on Its Own

A year-out goal, sitting by itself, is nearly useless — it's too big to act on today. This is the real engine of Part 3: reverse-engineering that big goal into something small enough to do this week, even today.

The method: break the one-year goal down in stages — year to nine months, nine to six, six to three, three to one month, one month to one week, one week to one day. In practice it's often easier to build the other direction — start from month one and work forward — since it's easier to picture a realistic starting point than to guess backward from a distant target.

## A Worked Example: The "Fight Club Body" Goal

Picture someone who says: "I want the lean, toned physique Brad Pitt had in Fight Club." Broken down, that goal needs four things: four to six workouts a week, three genuinely healthy on-time meals a day, low stress, and seven to eight hours of sleep. Committed to all four for a year, that's plausibly achievable.

Now assume the realistic starting point: this person doesn't work out at all, eats erratically, is highly stressed, and sleeps 4-5 hours a night, often until 2 a.m. Telling them to instantly adopt the ideal routine sets them up to fail — instant transformation isn't how change works, outside genuine emergencies.

So the plan gets staged. Month 1: two workouts a week, six healthy meals a week, bedtime 15 minutes earlier. Month 2: sleep pushed 15 minutes earlier again, healthy meals roughly doubled, a daily 5-10 minute breathing practice added. Month 3: three workouts a week, around 14 healthy meals, breathing practice extended, sleep earlier again. Month 4 onward: a cardio day added, healthy meals climb further, sleep keeps inching earlier — until the person hits the full target and shifts into maintenance.

That monthly goal breaks down further into weekly checkpoints (by week 3, a trainer is hired and two sessions are happening; by week 1, at least one workout and one healthy meal) — and finally into daily actions: today's goal might just be researching gyms nearby or setting an earlier bedtime alarm.

Worth noting: if you have the means, hiring a coach rather than self-teaching everything from YouTube is usually the smarter use of your time — a good coach compresses months of trial and error into accountability you won't get alone.

## The Weekly Rhythm That Keeps This Alive

Every morning, write a short list of that day's goals — five to ten minutes, nothing elaborate. Those daily actions accumulate into your weekly goal. At week's end, check whether you hit it. Underestimated yourself? Make next week's target harder. Overestimated? Dial it back. This calibration teaches you your actual capacity, turns actions into habits, and makes your one-year goals feel steadily more achievable, because they were built with real thought, not guesswork.

This same process — one year down to a single day — applies to each of the four categories: health, relationships, career, finance.

## Takeaways: What to Do With This Right Now

1. **Pull out your Part 1 and Part 2 answers.** This framework works far better with that groundwork done.
2. **Write one realistic goal for each category** — health, relationships, career, finance — for the next year.
3. **Break your first goal into a first-month version** that's achievable, not your end-state ambition.
4. **Break that month into weekly checkpoints**, and today's actions into a short daily list.
5. **Review weekly**, and adjust difficulty based on what you actually accomplished — this is where progress compounds.
6. **Consider outside support where it matters** — a coach isn't a shortcut around the work, it just removes the time you'd waste figuring things out alone.

The goal isn't a perfect plan on day one. It's a system where every day nudges you closer to the year you actually want, instead of leaving it as a vague hope you never start.
