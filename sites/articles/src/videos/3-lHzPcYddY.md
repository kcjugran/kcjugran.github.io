# What CrossFit Gets Wrong (And What to Do Instead)

CrossFit is everywhere. Walk into most gyms today and you'll find a "box" running WODs, ring work, and Olympic lifts stacked into the same hour. It has built a genuinely strong community and got a lot of people moving who might never have picked up a barbell otherwise. But if you're thinking about starting CrossFit, or you're already deep into it, it's worth understanding where the method runs into trouble — not to dismiss it, but to train smarter within it, or around it.

## Where CrossFit Came From

CrossFit is Greg Glassman's creation. He was an ex-gymnast who looked at gymnastics, weightlifting, powerlifting, sprinting, and endurance running and asked a simple question: what if you combined all of it into one conditioning system that trains the whole body, all the time? The idea, in his own framing, was to build something like military-style, all-around fitness for the everyday person — broad, powerful conditioning rather than one narrow skill.

Structurally, though, CrossFit is basically circuit training: a chain of exercises done for a set time or rep count, with a set rest period, moving from one movement to the next. Circuit training itself is decades old — that part isn't new. What CrossFit did well was popularize access. Gymnastics rings, Olympic lifting technique, and other fairly obscure, specialized training modalities became something you could walk into a regular gym and learn from a coach, when before that kind of instruction wasn't easy to find. That access is a real benefit.

## Problem One: Training Like It's a Competition

The core issue is that CrossFit treats everyday training the same way it treats competition. What you do in a CrossFit Games event is close to what you're asked to do in a regular class WOD. Program design normally follows a science: you decide what you're training for, then build a plan around it. CrossFit often skips that step. The implicit rule becomes "give me a hard workout with exercises I've never done, make every session different, make me sweat and empty the tank." That's not the same thing as a program built around a goal.

## Problem Two: Mixing Strength and Endurance Without a Plan

Your body adapts specifically to what you ask of it. Lifting 100kg and deadlifting builds a very different capacity than doing light reps for a long duration — these are different physiological adaptations. Strength and endurance are genuinely separate qualities, and training them heavily in the same session, over and over, works against you: heavy endurance volume tends to blunt strength gains. CrossFit's typical structure doesn't ask "are we building strength this month, or endurance, or muscle?" — it tries to hit everything at once, which sends the body mixed signals instead of a clear adaptation target.

## Problem Three: Not Enough Consistency to Actually Improve

Getting stronger or better at a specific lift requires a certain amount of repeated exposure — similar rep ranges, similar sets, similar movement, session after session. If you back squat heavy for 5 reps on Monday, do 20-rep squats Wednesday, and bodyweight squats Friday, you're giving your body wildly inconsistent signals. That's not a training block, it's chaos — and the body doesn't adapt cleanly to chaos. A real training block typically spends a stretch of weeks with similar exercises (back squat, then progression variations) so you can actually track and drive improvement, rather than chasing constant novelty.

## Problem Four: Skills That Need Focus, Not Variety

Complex movements — Olympic lifts, gymnastics rings — need frequent, focused practice to learn safely. You can't do Olympic weightlifting once and then not touch it again for two weeks and expect competence. If you want to be good at these, you need to train them often, which means your workouts will look similar for a while. That runs against CrossFit's built-in appetite for constant variety, and it's part of why technical skill and injury-free longevity tend to go together only when athletes quietly train less "randomly" than the class format suggests.

## Problem Five: A Culture Built Around Competing

CrossFit's culture leans on competition even in day-to-day training, and that mindset during training can be counterproductive for development. Training should mostly be driven by intrinsic motivation — trying to do a little better than yesterday — with enough self-respect to pull back or rest when something hurts. Comparing training to a group of people reading before an exam: some read together productively, but some who really needed a beginner-friendly approach ended up following others into an approach that didn't suit them, sometimes getting hurt in the process.

## Basics Before Advanced Moves

Before layering in advanced skills, foundational strength and control need to be in place. If someone can't do 20–30 solid push-ups, ring push-ups (which demand a lot of shoulder stabilization) won't offer much benefit and can add unnecessary risk. Same logic elsewhere: if someone can't hold a plank for a reasonable amount of time, loading them up with more advanced core work is likely to cause lower back pain rather than progress. Skipping fundamentals to chase harder-looking exercises is a mentality that doesn't serve anyone long-term.

## Takeaways

- CrossFit's core structure is circuit training combined with strength and gymnastics work — not a radical invention, but it did make specialized training more accessible.
- Its biggest flaw is treating regular training like competition, without a clear underlying program design.
- Mixing heavy strength and endurance work without separating training blocks blunts strength gains.
- Real improvement needs consistency: similar exercises, similar rep/set ranges, sustained over weeks — not constant novelty.
- Advanced skills (Olympic lifts, ring work) require focused, repeated practice, not one-off exposure.
- Master the basics first — push-ups before ring push-ups, a solid plank before loaded core work — to avoid injury and make faster progress.
- Whether you do CrossFit or any other training system, apply basic program design principles, and if you coach others, keep learning as much as you train.
