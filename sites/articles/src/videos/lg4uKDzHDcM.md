# The Best Suryanamaskar Variation for a 5-Minute Full-Body Warm-Up

Most of us know the feeling: you want to train, but a proper 15-20 minute warm-up feels like a luxury you don't have time for. The good news is that a warm-up doesn't need to be long to be effective — it needs to be well-designed. This flowing, floor-based variation on Suryanamaskar (Sun Salutation) hits nearly every major muscle group and joint in about five minutes, using continuous, connected movement instead of isolated stretches.

The sequence moves through a deadlift hinge, a crawl into push-up position, a Superman-style extension, a lunge with a rotational stretch, and a downward-dog-style hamstring and calf stretch — then repeats on the other side. It looks a little complicated the first time you see it, but once you understand the checkpoints, it becomes intuitive.

## Starting Position: The Deadlift Hinge

The movement begins standing, in a position similar to a deadlift setup. Push the hips back while reaching the hands forward, hinging until your torso is roughly at a 90-degree angle to your legs. From here, rotate the arms and draw them back, stretching the elbows upward. This rotation warms up the rear shoulder muscles (the posterior deltoids and upper back) before you've even left standing position.

## Hinge and Reach Forward

Holding that hinged position, bring the hands forward again, reaching toward the floor. This stretches the entire posterior chain — lower back and hamstrings together — setting up the transition into the floor-based portion of the flow.

## Crawling into Push-Up Position

From the forward reach, crawl the hands out in front of you until you arrive in a push-up position. From here, lower down under control — a negative push-up — keeping the movement slow rather than dropping.

## The Superman Extension

Once down, reach the arms forward and lift both the arms and legs off the ground into a Superman position. This activates the muscles running along the entire back — the posterior chain again, but now from the floor. From here, rotate the arms backward, mirroring the same rotation used in the opening hinge, which further warms the rear shoulders. Bring the arms back forward and lower down.

## Push-Up to Lunge Transition

Press back up through a push-up (the concentric portion, reversing the earlier negative), and from the top of the push-up, step one foot forward into a lunge position.

## Lunge Rotation and T-Spine Stretch

In the lunge, with the front leg forward, reach the same-side hand up toward the ceiling — this opens up the thoracic spine (T-spine) with a rotational stretch. Then bend the lower arm and draw the stretch forward and across the body slightly.

## Inner Thigh Opener

From the lunge, push the front knee outward, away from the body. This targets the inner thigh (adductors) with a deep, functional-angle stretch — a spot that's easy to neglect in a typical warm-up but matters for anyone doing squats, lunges, or rotational movement.

## Downward Dog and Hamstring Walk-Out

Next, walk the feet back into a downward-dog-style position, keeping the knees as straight as comfortable to deepen the hamstring and calf stretch. From here, walk the hands toward the feet, intensifying the hamstring stretch while trying to keep the knees straight and the back flat.

## Squat and Stand

Grab hold of the feet or ankles and sit down into a squat position, keeping the back straight and using the position to open the hips and stretch through that lowered range. Doing this once or twice helps you settle into the bottom position with better control. From the squat, reach forward into a final stretch, then reverse the whole movement back up to standing.

## Repeat on the Other Side

The full sequence — hinge, crawl, push-up, Superman, lunge, T-spine rotation, inner thigh stretch, downward dog, squat, stand — is then repeated leading with the opposite leg and arm, so both sides of the body get the same warm-up.

## Takeaways

- This is one continuous flow, not a series of static stretches — it moves from a standing deadlift hinge into a push-up position, a Superman extension, a rotational lunge, and a downward-dog hamstring stretch before returning to standing.
- The shoulder rotation (elbows driving up and back) shows up twice in the sequence — once standing, once from the Superman position — and is what warms up the rear shoulders and upper back.
- The lunge portion doubles as a T-spine rotation and an inner-thigh opener, covering two commonly tight areas in one position.
- The whole sequence is performed on both sides, so don't skip the second half.
- It's normal for this to feel complicated on the first attempt. Watching it two or three times before trying it, as suggested in the video, will make the transitions click faster.
- Done consistently, this full-body flow can replace a much longer traditional warm-up without leaving any major muscle group cold.
