# Can't Sleep at Night? Here's What's Actually Going Wrong

Most of us treat sleep as an afterthought — something that happens after the "real" work of the day is done. But if you've been waking up groggy, tossing for hours before drifting off, or running on caffeine just to function, the problem usually isn't willpower. It's that a few basic, fixable habits are quietly working against your body's natural rhythm.

Here's a practical breakdown of what good sleep actually looks like, why bad sleep costs you more than you think, and what you can change tonight.

## What Good Sleep Actually Looks Like

Good sleep isn't just about hours logged in bed — though duration matters. Most people need somewhere between 7 and 9 hours a night, with some doing fine on 7 and elite athletes sometimes needing closer to 9 or 10 because of how much physical recovery their bodies demand.

Beyond duration, quality shows up in a few clear signs: you don't wake up repeatedly through the night, you don't spend a long time lying there before falling asleep, you don't dream excessively or restlessly, and when you do wake up, it feels like the night passed quickly — almost like you blinked and morning arrived. You wake up refreshed, your usual tensions feel smaller, and you have real energy to take on the day.

Bad sleep is the mirror image: waking up multiple times, struggling to fall asleep in the first place, restless or interrupted rest, and dragging yourself out of bed still tired.

## Why It Matters More Than You Think

Poor sleep isn't just about feeling tired. Over time it shows up in places you might not expect:

- **Mood and mental clarity** — concentration suffers and irritability creeps in.
- **Immunity** — your body's defenses weaken.
- **Skin health** — sleep-deprived skin visibly suffers.
- **Eyes** — eye strain and related issues become more likely.
- **Weight** — when you don't sleep enough, hormone production that regulates hunger gets disrupted, so you end up hungrier and eating past your normal signals.
- **Recovery** — if you work out, poor sleep directly shows up as worse recovery. Things that should feel easy start feeling much harder, both physically and mentally.
- **Long-term health** — research links chronically poor sleep to reduced lifespan.

The throughline is simple: sleep is when your body and mind actually repair themselves. Cut that process short consistently, and everything downstream — mood, immunity, fitness, recovery — pays the price.

## Work With Your Body's Clock, Not Against It

Your body runs on a circadian rhythm — an internal clock tuned to the rising and setting of the sun, refined over an extremely long evolutionary history. It expects consistency: sleep at a certain time, wake at a certain time. Modern life — irregular sleep times, late nights, screens glowing until 2 a.m. — throws that rhythm off, and the body simply doesn't recover as well when its signals are scrambled.

A simple target: try to be asleep by 10–10:30 p.m., and aim for 7–8 hours. This isn't about rigid rules — it's about giving your body the consistency it's built for. If you can't hit that exactly because of shift work or other constraints, the takeaway is still the same: consistency matters more than any one "perfect" bedtime.

## Breathe Through Your Nose

One underrated detail: how you breathe at night changes how well you sleep. Mouth breathing tends to activate the body, while nasal breathing signals it to relax. If you sleep with your mouth open, you may be working against your own rest without realizing it. A simple fix some people use is a light strip of surgical tape over the lips (never fully sealing the nose) to encourage nasal breathing through the night — worth trying if you suspect this is an issue for you.

## Mind Your Meal Timing

What and when you eat before bed matters more than most people assume. Try to finish eating 2–3 hours before you actually lie down. Eating too close to bedtime means your body is still digesting when it should be winding down — this shows up the next morning as sluggishness and a digestive system that hasn't caught up. Eating right before sleep can also lead to more restless, vivid dreams.

If you do eat close to bedtime, a meal slightly higher in carbohydrates (like rice or roti) can actually help — carbs support the release of compounds that promote sleepiness. A warm glass of turmeric milk before bed is also a familiar home remedy — milk contains tryptophan, which supports the production of sleep-related hormones.

## Put the Phone Down — Literally

Phones and other electronic devices emit small amounts of electromagnetic radiation, and while research on the effects is still developing, the practical advice is straightforward: keep your phone off or on airplane mode, turn off Wi-Fi, and physically keep devices away from your bed — ideally several feet away — rather than under your pillow or on the nightstand. If you need an alarm, set it and then put the phone across the room. This also has the side benefit of removing the temptation to scroll social media right before sleep, which itself works against a calm, sleep-ready mind.

## Empty Your Mind Before You Try to Empty Your Body

A habit worth building: before bed, write down whatever is on your mind — worries, to-dos, things you're grateful for, whatever needs to come out of your head and onto paper. It doesn't need structure; a simple journal or even a loose brain-dump works. Getting it out of your head and onto the page makes it much easier to actually relax once you're lying down.

## Try Simple Breathing Techniques

Two breathing patterns are worth practicing lying in bed:

1. **Box breathing** — inhale, hold, exhale, hold, each for a few seconds, repeated for 5–10 minutes until your body visibly relaxes.
2. **4-7-8 breathing** — inhale for 4 seconds, hold for 7, exhale for 8. This one is intense — it can genuinely feel like your body is being forced into a deeply relaxed state — but a few rounds of it can be remarkably effective at pulling you toward sleep.

## A Word on Supplements

Most people reaching for a "sleep fix" don't actually need a supplement — the lifestyle basics above solve the vast majority of sleep problems. But if you want extra support, a few natural options are worth knowing:

- **Lavender oil** — a drop mixed into a carrier oil and rubbed on the wrists or applied lightly before bed. No real downside, and many people find it genuinely relaxing.
- **Caffeine awareness** — treat caffeine itself as something to manage like a supplement: too much late in the day undermines sleep, so moderate your intake and cut it off well before bedtime.
- **Calming herbal drinks** — a warm, caffeine-free drink before bed can help signal wind-down time.
- **Magnesium** — commonly used to support better sleep quality.
- **Melatonin — use with real caution.** Melatonin is the hormone your body naturally produces to signal it's time to sleep. Taking it as a supplement can help in the short term, but your body can respond by reducing its own natural production, since it's now getting melatonin from outside. Lean on it regularly and you risk becoming dependent on the pill just to fall asleep at all — and if you stop, sleep can get worse than before you started, because your body's natural production has been suppressed. If you use it, keep it occasional (like for jet lag), at low doses, and ideally with medical guidance — not as a nightly habit.

## Takeaways: What To Actually Do Tonight

- **Set a consistent target bedtime**, ideally lights-out by 10–10:30 p.m., aiming for 7–8 hours.
- **Finish eating 2–3 hours before bed**; if eating late is unavoidable, favor a lighter, carb-leaning meal.
- **Power down your devices** — phone off or on airplane mode, Wi-Fi off, phone physically away from your bed.
- **Breathe through your nose**, not your mouth, while you sleep.
- **Write before you sleep** — a few minutes of journaling to clear your head.
- **Practice a breathing technique** — box breathing or 4-7-8 — for 5–10 minutes lying in bed.
- **Treat supplements as optional support, not a fix** — and be especially cautious with melatonin; it's not meant for nightly long-term use.

None of this requires buying anything or overhauling your life overnight. Pick two or three of these and apply them consistently for a couple of weeks — sleep quality responds to consistency far more than to any single trick.
