# Know Your Dark Side: The Basics of Shadow Work

Most of us are fairly comfortable describing ourselves as good people. We help strangers, care about our families, try to be kind. But every so often, something slips through the cracks — a flash of rage at someone we love, a petty thought we'd never say out loud, an urge we quickly bury and pretend never happened. That uncomfortable residue is the starting point for shadow work.

## What the Shadow Actually Is

The shadow is the part of your mind you don't look at directly — the part you've folded away, denied, or pushed down because it doesn't fit the picture of who you want to be. It isn't some exotic psychological affliction. It's ordinary: the irritation you swallow, the impulse you don't act on, the thought you'd be embarrassed to admit. Because it stays hidden, you often can't see it operating in your own life, even when it's shaping your decisions.

Consider the classic pattern of someone who stays in an abusive relationship. From the outside, the way out looks obvious. From the inside, a hidden part of the mind — a shadow belief about worth, safety, or loyalty — keeps overriding the obvious choice. Or think of someone caught in a cycle of gambling and denial, insisting each time that they've quit, that this time is different, while a part of themselves they refuse to look at keeps steering the wheel. In both cases, the person isn't lying for effect — they genuinely can't see the part of themselves that's in control. That's the defining feature of shadow material: it works on you precisely because you can't perceive it.

## Two Ways to Start Locating It

The video offers two practical entry points.

**Meditation.** Sit quietly, turn your attention inward, and begin noticing your own patterns and reactions without judgment. It's a slower, more internal method — sit with yourself and let what's hidden gradually surface.

**Ask the people who know you.** This one takes more courage. Pick three to five people who are genuinely close to you — friends, family, people who see you honestly. Ask them directly: what are the things about me that, if I changed them, would make me better? What flaws do you see that hold my personality back? Then compare the answers. Look for the traits that repeat across multiple people — those overlaps are a strong signal you've found a real blind spot, not just one person's opinion.

## Questions to Surface Your Own Shadow

Beyond those two methods, the video offers a set of specific questions designed to shine a light into the blind spot directly:

- **What do you hate about other people?** The traits that irritate you most in others are often the very traits you've suppressed in yourself. If someone who talks too much bothers you disproportionately, it may be because you were once shamed for talking too much and shut that part of yourself down.
- **What topics make you uncomfortable or make you change the subject?** Common examples raised in the video are death and money — subjects many people avoid discussing honestly because facing them stirs something unresolved.
- **What are you ashamed of?** Make a private list of the things you'd hate for someone to bring up about you. Shame is one of the clearest markers of shadow material — though the video also notes an important distinction: something you're aware of and consciously managing (like knowing you have a sweet tooth but keeping it in check) isn't shadow. It only becomes shadow when you deny it exists at all.
- **What would you take even at someone else's expense?** This question exposes the parts of yourself you'd rather believe don't exist — the self-interest you don't like to admit to.
- **What negative traits in others trigger the strongest reaction in you?** The video uses the example of someone who "doesn't listen to anyone" and does their own thing — and notes that reaction often masks a hidden envy of that same freedom, a freedom you may have denied yourself.

## The Two Sides of Every Trait

One useful reframe from the video: almost every trait you dislike in yourself or others has both a cost and a benefit. Not listening to others can mean being inconsiderate — but it also means faster decisions and less second-guessing. Listening carefully to others builds trust and connection — but can also slow you down and lead you to override your own instincts to please the group. Shadow work isn't about eliminating traits; it's about seeing both sides clearly enough to choose consciously instead of reacting blindly.

## Why This Matters

The video's closing framing is direct: every person carries both the "god" and the "devil" within them. Everyone wants to display the god — the good, admirable parts. But until you can also see the devil — the denied, hidden parts — that hidden side doesn't disappear. It keeps running your life from behind the scenes, unseen and therefore unmanaged. Genuine self-acceptance, the video suggests, has to include the parts of yourself you'd rather not look at.

## Takeaways

- The shadow isn't pathology — it's the ordinary, denied, or suppressed material every person carries.
- You can't fix or integrate what you refuse to see; the first task is simply becoming aware.
- Two practical starting points: sit with yourself through meditation, or ask three to five people close to you what they honestly see.
- Use targeted questions — what you hate in others, what shames you, what topics you avoid, what you'd gain at someone else's expense — to locate specific blind spots.
- Notice the overlap when multiple people point to the same trait; that repetition is a signal worth taking seriously.
- Remember that most traits carry both a cost and a benefit — the goal isn't to erase your shadow, but to see it clearly enough to choose your response instead of being run by it.
