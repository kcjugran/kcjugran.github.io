# Why You Can't Meditate

If you've tried meditation and given up, you're not broken — you're probably just using the wrong style. That's the starting point of this piece: meditation isn't one technique. It's an umbrella term covering hundreds of different practices, some going back thousands of years in Indian scripture, long before Western medical science began studying them seriously. Because it's so old and so varied, what "meditation" means ends up depending entirely on who you ask. The struggle most beginners feel isn't a personal failing — it's a mismatch between the method they picked and who they actually are.

## There Is No One "Correct" Meditation

The core claim here is simple but easy to forget: virtually every meditation technique works, if you practice it consistently over a long period of time. The problem isn't that meditation doesn't work on you. It's that most people pick a style at random — often whatever's trending — sit with it for a few days, struggle, and conclude meditation "isn't for them." The fix isn't more willpower. It's choosing a practice that fits your mental and physical temperament so the process feels less like a fight.

## Match the Style to the Person

Different techniques suit different nervous systems and personalities:

- **Silent, still practices** (like Vipassana, where practitioners go quiet and observant for extended periods) work well for people who can sit with stillness and don't get restless easily.
- **Movement-based practices** suit people who find pure stillness difficult — those who "can't sit still" may do better with a moving meditative form than a seated one.
- **Practices channeling stronger emotion** — even something like directed anger or intensity — can suit people who don't respond well to purely calm, passive approaches.

The advice isn't to force yourself into whichever style is most popular. It's to try a few, notice which one reduces your inner resistance, and build consistency around that one. One reference point mentioned is a book cataloguing something like 120 different meditation styles — the point being: options exist, and Osho's advice is essentially "try them and see what suits your inner self."

## The Brainwave Argument

There's a science-facing explanation offered too: meditation is described as shifting your brain's activity into different wave states — alpha, theta, delta — away from the fast, high-speed mental "traffic" most of us run on during a normal, busy day. When your mind is already moving fast from constant activity and stimulation, dropping into a slow, settled state is a bigger jump — which is part of why meditation can feel so hard for people whose days are packed and overstimulating.

## Why Mornings Work Better For Beginners

This is one of the more practical, concrete points in the video: early morning is recommended as the easiest time to start a meditation practice. The reasoning given is that at night, right before sleep, your mind naturally slows down and relaxes — and that slower state carries over into the morning. Your mind is already "low and slow" when you wake up, which makes it far easier to slip into meditation then than after a full day of activity has sped your mind back up. If mornings genuinely aren't possible, doing it after some calming activity later in the day is also fine — but mornings are the low-hanging fruit for beginners.

## Time, Place, and Consistency

Beyond brainwave states and style-matching, three practical anchors are emphasized:

1. **Fixed time and location.** Keep both consistent — same time of day, same spot — rather than meditating wherever and whenever convenience allows.
2. **Silence your phone.** Simple, obvious, but stated directly: notifications and sounds break the state you're trying to build.
3. **Let the location "absorb" the practice.** This is a subtler, more psychological point — the idea that a space you consistently meditate in starts to carry an association with stillness. Compare it to how sitting on your bed makes you feel like doing nothing, purely from repeated association. The claim is that meditating in the same spot repeatedly conditions your mind to relax faster there over time — essentially a psychological anchor, not a mystical one.

## A Note on Spirituality and Religion

The video deliberately steers around spirituality and religion as topics, explaining that both carry a lot of attachment and quick emotional reaction — people can get defensive or dogmatic fast ("this is what my guru taught, so this is the only right way"). Rather than wade into that territory, the advice is to keep exploring meditation on your own terms, using your own discernment, rather than outsourcing the answer to any single authority or tradition.

## Takeaways

- Meditation is not one practice — there are hundreds of styles, and no single one is "the right one" for everyone.
- Almost every technique works if practiced consistently over a long stretch of time; inconsistency, not the technique itself, is usually the real obstacle.
- Choose a style that matches your temperament: stillness-based for those who can sit calmly, movement-based for those who can't, and so on.
- Mornings are the easiest entry point for beginners because your mind is naturally slower and calmer right after waking.
- Keep your meditation time, location, and phone habits consistent — repetition trains both your mind and your environment to support the practice.
- Explore different styles with curiosity rather than dogma, especially when spiritual or religious framing is involved — stay open rather than rigid about "the one true way."
