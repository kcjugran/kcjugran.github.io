# How to Breathe Correctly: Understanding Diaphragmatic Breathing

You've probably heard it before: breathe from your belly, not your chest. It's become common wellness advice, and for good reason — but knowing the rule and actually practicing it correctly are two different things. Most people, without realizing it, breathe by lifting and dropping their chest with every inhale and exhale. It feels normal simply because it's familiar. But this chest-dominant pattern comes with a hidden cost, and learning to recognize it is the first step toward breathing the way your body was designed to.

## The Chest-Breathing Trap

A simple way to notice your own pattern is to lie down and pay attention to what moves first when you inhale. If your chest rises and falls with each breath, that's a strong signal you're breathing from the chest rather than the diaphragm. This isn't just an inefficient habit — it actively tightens the muscles around your neck, and over time that tension can carry over into the rest of your day. Chest breathing keeps the neck engaged in a job it wasn't primarily built for, and the result is often a low background tension that many people carry without ever connecting it back to how they breathe.

## What Diaphragmatic Breathing Actually Feels Like

The alternative is to let the breath fill the belly first, the way a balloon fills with air. Picture your stomach as that balloon: as you inhale, it's not just the front of your belly that expands — the air should spread into the sides of your torso and into your lower back as well. This is sometimes described as three-dimensional breathing, because the expansion isn't limited to one direction. It happens outward, sideways, and backward, almost simultaneously, rather than just as a single rise at the front of the stomach.

In practice, this can take some getting used to. Certain areas of the torso will be easy to expand into right away, while others may feel stiff or unresponsive at first. That's normal — the goal isn't to force an even expansion instantly, but to bring awareness to each area and practice filling it gradually. Over time, with attention, the sides and lower back begin to respond the same way the front of the belly already does.

## Practicing the Pattern

A useful way to build this awareness is to place your hands on the sides of your torso and simply feel what happens as you breathe. As you inhale, notice the stomach rising forward, like the balloon filling. As you exhale, notice the release. The same principle can then be applied while standing or moving — not just lying down — so that the pattern becomes something you can carry into daily life rather than something reserved for a mat or a quiet room.

It's worth noting that even with a well-developed diaphragmatic breath, a small amount of air will still move into the chest — and that's fine. The proportion matters more than perfection: most of each breath, roughly three-quarters or more, should still be filling the belly and torso, with only a smaller share reaching the chest. The goal isn't to eliminate chest movement entirely, but to shift where the majority of the work happens.

## Why This Matters

This approach draws on ideas associated with corrective exercise specialist Paul Chek, whose work on breathing mechanics has influenced how many practitioners think about the diaphragm's role in posture, tension, and overall function. The core idea is straightforward: your breathing pattern isn't neutral. It either feeds tension into your neck and upper body, or it supports a more relaxed, efficient way of moving through your day. Once you start paying attention to which one you're doing, it becomes hard to unnotice.

This is also why diaphragmatic breathing tends to come up again and again in wellness and body-awareness conversations — it isn't a minor technical detail. Something as automatic and constant as breathing, done thousands of times a day without a second thought, has an outsized effect on how tense or at ease your body feels. Shifting that one pattern is a small, quiet change with a very large footprint over time.

## Takeaways

- Chest-dominant breathing tightens the neck and can build tension over time, even though it often goes unnoticed.
- Diaphragmatic breathing fills the belly like a balloon — not just forward, but into the sides and lower back too, in a three-dimensional pattern.
- Some areas of the torso will be easier to expand into than others at first; awareness and practice gradually even this out.
- A small amount of chest movement during breathing is normal — most of the breath should still be reaching the belly and torso.
- Practice noticing the pattern with your hands on your sides, then carry that awareness into breathing throughout the day, not just during dedicated practice.
