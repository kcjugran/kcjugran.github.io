# Insomnia, Oversleeping, or Can't Wake Up? Here's the Simple Fix

If you struggle to fall asleep, sleep too much, or can't seem to drag yourself out of bed in the morning, the problem usually isn't willpower. It's your circadian rhythm — the internal sleep-wake cycle that's tied directly to sunlight. This cycle decides when you feel energetic, when you feel sluggish, and when your body starts signaling that it's time to wind down. For most of human history, that signal came from the sun itself: bright light at midday, softening light in the evening, and darkness by night. Our bodies still have photosensitive cells that respond to exactly this pattern.

The problem is that modern life has broken the signal. We spend the day indoors under artificial light, then come home in the evening to harsh, bright bulbs and screens that stay on well past midnight. Your body has no way of knowing the sun has gone down, so it never gets the cue to start producing the sleep hormones that make you drowsy. The fix isn't complicated — it's about rebuilding that lost connection to natural light and rhythm.

## Rethink Your Evening Light

Start reducing your light exposure at least an hour or two before bed — ideally more. Swap harsh overhead lighting for something soft: a dim lamp, a warm-toned bulb, even candlelight. This kind of lighting doesn't interfere with your body's ability to relax and wind down the way bright white or blue-toned light does.

Keep your phone away from you in the hours before sleep. Screens emit a lot of blue light, and the content itself — whether it's short videos, political arguments, or anything that gets you emotionally worked up — keeps your mind agitated right when it should be settling down.

## Replace Screen Time With Something Calmer

Instead of scrolling before bed, pick up a good book — fiction or something educational that feeds your mind well. A physical book has no light of its own, so it doesn't disrupt your body's wind-down process, and reading actually engages your imagination in a gentle, calming way.

If reading isn't your thing, try listening instead. Lo-fi music, jazz, or an audiobook can serve the same purpose — something calm and enriching your subconscious can absorb as you drift off, without the stimulation of a screen.

## Talk to Your Future Self

One simple, almost strange-sounding technique: before you fall asleep, repeat an intention to yourself about the time you want to wake up. Say it with confidence, several times, as if it's already decided — something like telling yourself you'll wake up at 5:30 the next morning. This kind of mental rehearsal appears to genuinely shift when people wake up, priming the mind ahead of time.

## Use Your Breath to Wake Up Energized

For mornings when you're groggy, try a short, energizing breathing exercise similar to Kapalbhati, a yogic breathing practice. It's not about deep, forceful breathing — it uses quick contractions of the stomach, breathing out through the mouth, rather than the chest. Keep the pace manageable; going too hard will tire you out rather than energize you. This kind of active breathing gives your body a jolt that helps shake off morning grogginess.

A related and slightly unusual habit worth trying: taping your mouth shut while you sleep so you're forced to breathe through your nose. Mouth breathing at night — especially heavy or open-mouth breathing — tends to be more stressful on the body, while nasal breathing supports deeper, calmer rest. If you don't have a deviated septum or similar issue, many people notice a real difference in sleep quality after trying this.

## Bringing It Together

None of this requires special equipment or a big lifestyle overhaul. It comes down to a few consistent habits: dim your lights and put away screens in the evening, replace scrolling with reading or calm audio, gently program your mind before sleep with a clear wake-up intention, and consider a short breathing practice — both to fall asleep more easily and to wake up with more energy. And, as always, aim for a full night's sleep, since rest is one of the most powerful and freely available tools you have for your health.

## Takeaways

- Your sleep-wake cycle is driven by light exposure — modern indoor and screen lighting disrupts the natural cues your body relies on.
- Dim your lights and put your phone away in the hour or two before bed; swap bright bulbs for soft or candlelight.
- Replace pre-sleep screen time with reading a good book or listening to calm music or an audiobook.
- Before sleep, repeat a clear intention about your wake-up time to help "program" your mind.
- A brief, gentle Kapalbhati-style breathing exercise can help shake off morning grogginess — don't overdo the pace.
- Nasal breathing (including trying mouth taping, if it's safe for you) may improve sleep quality compared to mouth breathing.
- Prioritize a full night's sleep — it's described as one of the most powerful, free tools for overall health.
