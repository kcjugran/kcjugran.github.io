# Should Everyone Take Supplements? Yes — Here's Why

It's a small, mildly controversial topic, but it's one worth answering plainly: should everyone take supplements? Yes. Not instead of food — alongside it. Here's the reasoning, and what that actually looks like in practice.

## Food First, Always

Let's be clear about what this isn't. This isn't a case for replacing meals with pills, or treating supplements as a shortcut around eating well. Your diet is still the foundation. No amount of supplementation makes up for a genuinely poor way of eating. If anything, someone who eats badly and expects a bottle of vitamins to fix it is missing the point entirely.

So why bring supplements into the conversation at all if food comes first? Because in today's world, food alone often isn't enough to fully cover what our bodies need — not because people are eating "wrong" in some moral sense, but because the food itself has changed.

## The Quality of Food Has Quietly Declined

This is the heart of the argument. Over the past several decades, the nutrient density of common fruits and vegetables has dropped — a shift driven by soil depletion, intensive farming, and the broader environmental load of chemicals and pollution our food systems now carry. The soil that grows our produce has been pushed harder and given less time to recover, and the produce reflects that.

The practical result: to get the same nutritional value that a piece of fruit or a clove of garlic used to provide a few decades ago, you'd need to eat several times as much of it today. That's not a reason to despair over your grocery list — it's simply a reason to recognize that "eating your fruits and vegetables" doesn't carry the same nutritional guarantee it once did.

On top of that, there's the more familiar problem: most people don't eat enough, or don't eat well enough, consistently. Between processed convenience foods and inconsistent habits, the average diet is carrying an even bigger nutritional gap than the soil issue alone would suggest.

## It Depends on Your Life, Not Just Your Diet

One important nuance: supplement needs aren't one-size-fits-all, even among people eating similarly. It comes down to your activity level and how demanding your lifestyle is — mentally and physically.

Someone with a sedentary, low-demand routine needs less support than someone who is constantly active, training hard, or under heavy mental and physical load. The higher the output your life requires, the more support your body needs to keep up — supplementation included.

It also matters how much control you have over your food. Someone who can shop fresh, cook at home, and consistently eat high-quality meals is naturally going to need less supplemental support than someone relying on inconsistent or lower-quality food sources. That's not a judgment — it's just how the math works. Good food access lowers the gap; it doesn't erase it.

## A Reasonable Starting Point

Rather than a long, specific protocol, the video points to a few broad categories most people are likely underserved on:

- **Magnesium** — commonly under-consumed, and something most people benefit from having consistently.
- **A high-quality multivitamin** — a baseline layer of coverage for the everyday micronutrient gaps that modern food quality leaves behind.
- **A high-quality omega-3 source** — supporting the essential fats that are easy to fall short on.
- **Sunlight exposure** — not a supplement in a bottle, but a genuine, often-overlooked input. Getting real sun exposure on skin matters and shouldn't be replaced by a pill when it's available naturally.

The emphasis throughout is on quality over quantity — fewer, better-sourced additions rather than a shelf full of products. And there's an openness to going deeper: if there's interest, a more detailed, specific breakdown of supplementation strategy is something worth covering in a follow-up.

## The Bigger Picture

There's also a broader point tucked into this: choosing quality food and quality supplementation is, in part, a vote for the industries doing things well — the growers, producers, and brands prioritizing real nutrition over volume. Supporting that side of the food system is part of the long-term solution, even while supplementation fills the gap in the meantime.

## Takeaways

- Supplements are a support system, not a food replacement — diet still comes first.
- Modern produce is measurably less nutrient-dense than it was decades ago, largely due to soil depletion and environmental factors.
- Most people fall short nutritionally not just from food quality, but from inconsistent eating habits.
- Your ideal supplement load depends on your activity level and how much control you have over eating well — not a fixed formula for everyone.
- A sensible baseline to consider: magnesium, a high-quality multivitamin, a quality omega-3, and real sunlight exposure.
- Prioritize quality and sourcing over quantity of products.
