# Too Busy for Your Passion? Try the 80/20 Trick

If your days are full but your passion is nowhere in them, you're not alone. Most of us run through a mental list of a hundred small tasks a day — cleaning, cooking, work deadlines, school pickups, errands — and by the time the list is done, there's no time or energy left for the thing we actually wanted to build. This isn't a discipline problem. It's a focus problem, and there's a simple mental model that can fix it.

## The 80/20 Principle, Applied to Your Day

The idea borrows from the Pareto Principle — the observation that a small slice of your effort tends to produce most of your results. Applied to a busy day, it looks like this: roughly 80% of what you do is necessary but low-leverage — the routine maintenance of life. The other 20% is where your energy, if placed well, can move your life forward in a real way.

The trick isn't to abandon the 80%. It's to stop treating all of your remaining time as interchangeable, and instead ask a sharper question: out of everything left in my day, which one thing would give me the most benefit if I focused on it?

## Finding Your "One Thing"

This is where the idea gets more useful than a generic productivity tip. It's not just about carving out free time — it's about identifying the single highest-leverage activity within that free time, and pointing your limited hours at it deliberately.

Take the example of someone who dreams of becoming a serious dancer but is just starting out. Four things matter here: getting better at the craft itself, connecting with other people in that world, building visibility, and doing it consistently. Trying to chase all four separately would spread you thin. But there's often one action that does double or triple duty — for instance, practicing a difficult new dance move and posting it. That single act builds skill, creates exposure, and invites connection from others in the same field, all at once. Four to five hours of focused practice a day, done consistently, adds up to real progress — far more than scattered, unfocused effort ever would.

The lesson: look for the one action in your day that quietly serves multiple goals at once. That's usually your highest-leverage 20%.

## The Homemaker Example

Consider a mother managing a household — cooking, cleaning, and caring for her family. These are necessary and, done with love, genuinely meaningful. But if a disproportionate amount of the day goes into one task, it's worth asking: is there a smaller, smarter version of this that still honors what matters, while freeing up space?

The suggestion here is practical, not dismissive: find a few good, ready-made meal options — maybe a little more expensive, but reliable and healthy — that can cover three to six days a week. This isn't about cutting corners on care; it's about protecting energy. When one recurring task stops draining the whole day, what opens up is space to pursue a hobby, a passion, or simply rest without guilt. The point isn't to do less for your family — it's to stop one necessary task from silently swallowing every spare hour.

## The Business Owner Example

For someone starting a business, the highest-leverage 20% often isn't more product development — it's marketing and branding. A good product on its own doesn't guarantee traction; visibility does. The video draws on experience from the fitness industry to make this point: mediocre products with strong marketing regularly outperform excellent products that nobody knows about. If you're investing your limited time or money into your business, personal branding and marketing deserve to be near the top of that list — alongside genuine innovation and quality — rather than an afterthought.

## Making It Practical

The method suggested is refreshingly simple: keep a running list. Every time you notice yourself completing a task or unlocking a small win, write it down. Do this consistently — even just noting things as they happen throughout the day — and by the next morning, you'll already have a clearer, evidence-based sense of what's actually moving your life forward versus what's just noise.

Two small companion habits are mentioned as well: taking a walk each morning, and keeping things simple rather than overcomplicating the process. Neither needs to be elaborate to be effective.

## Takeaways

- You don't need more hours — you need a clearer view of which activity in your day carries the most weight.
- Look for the "one thing" that serves several goals simultaneously (skill-building, visibility, and connection, for example) instead of chasing each one separately.
- For routine responsibilities that eat your day, look for a smaller, smarter version that still honors the people or standards involved — the goal is protecting energy, not cutting care.
- If you're building something (a business, a craft, a body of work), don't underinvest in visibility and branding — a good effort still needs to be seen to matter.
- Track your wins daily, even briefly. A simple record of what worked will show you, more honestly than guesswork, where your real 20% lives.
