# Understanding Training Injuries: The Capacity-Based Recovery Method

Most people think about injuries the wrong way. They ask, "What did I do wrong?" — as if there's always a bad rep, a bad angle, a moment of carelessness to point to. In this session, the coach behind Living Foundations pushes back on that idea and offers a simpler, more useful lens: injuries aren't about doing something "wrong." They're about doing something your joints and muscles weren't yet capable of handling.

This article walks through that framework — why injuries actually happen, why "form" gets blamed unfairly, and a practical system for training safely around an existing injury instead of stopping altogether.

## The Real Cause of Injury: Capacity vs. Load

The central idea is straightforward. Every joint and muscle has a certain capacity — a ceiling for how much load, stress, or range of motion it can currently handle. An injury happens when you place a demand on that tissue that exceeds its current capacity. That's the mechanism, every time.

To make the point, the coach uses a comparison: if two people attempt the exact same lift with the exact same form, one might walk away injured and the other might not feel a thing. The difference isn't technique — one person's joints and muscles had built up the capacity to absorb that load, and the other's hadn't yet. He extends this with a vivid example: an untrained finger bending under load versus a boxer's conditioned knuckles. The same motion that injures untrained tissue is manageable for tissue progressively built up to handle it. Capacity, not motion, is what determines whether you get hurt.

## Why "Bad Form" Isn't the Real Villain

This is where the video challenges a common fitness belief — that injuries happen because someone used bad form. The coach's position is that form isn't a "right or wrong" switch; whatever form you used, on that day, exceeded what your body could currently support. Poor form is often just another way of asking for more capacity than you had. Someone else, with better-trained joints and muscles, could do the "same" imperfect movement and be fine.

This isn't a license to ignore technique — it's a reframe. Instead of treating form as a moral failing ("I did it wrong"), the useful question becomes: was I asking my body to do something it wasn't yet ready for?

## So Why Do Injuries Happen During Training?

The video identifies overreaching as the practical, everyday cause of most training injuries: pushing weight, volume, or range of motion past what your joints and connective tissue can currently tolerate. Sometimes that's a one-time spike; other times it's cumulative — the same movement repeated past the point your tissue can recover from, session after session.

Individual capacity also varies a lot — genetics, training history, and joint structure all play into why one person tolerates a given exercise better than another. That's normal, not a reason to compare yourself unfavorably to someone else in the gym.

## Once You're Injured: The Three-Category Framework

The most actionable part of the video is a simple system for figuring out what you can still safely do once an injury has occurred, rather than shutting down training entirely. The coach breaks exercises for an injured area into three categories:

**1. Pain-free exercises** — movements involving the injured area (or nearby muscles and joints) that cause no pain at all, even under normal training conditions. Safe to keep doing as-is.

**2. Pain-later exercises** — movements that feel fine at first but start to cause discomfort as you accumulate volume (more reps, sets, or time under tension). Not automatically off-limits — dose carefully, stay below the point where pain shows up, and re-test every week or two to track whether tolerance is improving.

**3. Pain-throughout exercises** — movements that hurt from the very first rep and continue hurting the entire time. Set these aside during the acute recovery period; continuing to load them past a real pain signal works against recovery.

The practical instruction: go through your normal exercise list, one movement at a time, and sort each into these three buckets — including trying variations, since a slight change in setup can sometimes move something from "painful" into "pain-free."

## Using Pain as Feedback, Not a Stop Sign

A manageable amount of discomfort is not automatically a red flag. The coach describes using a rough personal scale (roughly 1–2 out of 10) as an acceptable threshold for pain-later exercises — mild, controllable sensation is different from sharp or escalating pain. The goal isn't "no pain no gain," and it isn't "zero discomfort ever" either. It's staying below the threshold where you're causing further damage, while keeping blood flow moving to the area, which the video frames as genuinely helpful for recovery.

Re-check pain-later exercises regularly — roughly weekly — rather than assuming tolerance is fixed. As recovery progresses, exercises often shift categories: something that used to hurt from the first rep may become pain-later, then eventually pain-free.

The conversation also touches briefly on nutrition as part of the recovery picture — worth treating as part of the same process as your training plan, not a separate concern.

## Practical Takeaways

- **Injuries come from a capacity gap, not a moral failing.** The load exceeded what your joints and muscles could handle — more often the real cause than "bad form" alone.
- **Build capacity gradually.** Progressive, consistent training raises tolerance over time, for healthy and previously injured tissue alike.
- **Don't stop training the whole body over one injured area.** Sort exercises into pain-free, pain-later, and pain-throughout, and keep training what doesn't aggravate it.
- **Use pain-later exercises carefully** — stay below the point pain starts, and re-test weekly.
- **Avoid pushing through pain-throughout movements** during the acute phase.
- **Track pain honestly** on a simple personal scale to tell manageable discomfort apart from a genuine warning sign.

This is general training information based on the concepts discussed in the video, not medical advice. If you're dealing with a significant or persistent injury, please consult a doctor or physical therapist before deciding how to train around it.
