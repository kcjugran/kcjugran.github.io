# What Is the Wim Hof Method? A Simple Breathing Practice to Try Today

Wim Hof is a Dutch endurance athlete known for extraordinary feats of physical and mental resilience. Over years of meditation and intense training, he developed a method that now carries his name. The full Wim Hof Method actually has two parts: the breathing technique, and cold exposure through ice immersion. This piece focuses on the breathing side — a practice you can start today, no ice bath required.

## The Two Halves of the Method

It's worth being upfront about scope. The ice immersion component is a distinct practice with its own considerations, and it isn't something to walk into casually. What follows here is the breathing pattern on its own, so that you can begin experiencing the method's effects without needing to jump into cold water first.

## Why Bother With the Breathing?

The reported benefits are fairly direct. Practiced in the morning, it's described as clearing grogginess and waking the body up. Done at night, it's linked to better sleep. Across the board, people report a noticeable increase in energy in the body. These are the kinds of effects that make the breathing half of the method worth trying on its own, independent of any cold exposure.

## The Breathing Pattern, Step by Step

The core cycle looks like this:

1. **Deep inhale, then a passive release (not a full exhale) — repeated.** You cycle through inhale, release, inhale, release for roughly 30 to 40 breaths.
2. **Notice the sensation.** After that many rounds, it's common to feel tingling, a kind of shakiness, or a "high" feeling in the body. This happens because you're pulling in significantly more oxygen than usual, which shifts your CO2 levels and makes the body feel unusual — that's expected, not a warning sign in itself.
3. **After the 30–40 breaths, take one long inhale, then exhale.**
4. **Hold the exhale** — for as long as feels comfortable. This could be 10, 20, 30, or 40 seconds, depending entirely on your own capacity. There's no fixed number; you go by what's comfortable for you.
5. **Then inhale again and hold that breath briefly before releasing.**

That's one full cycle. You then repeat the entire sequence — 30–40 breaths, exhale, hold, inhale, hold — for three to five rounds total, again depending on what you can comfortably sustain. Aim for at least three rounds if you can; five to ten is fine too for those who want more.

### The Final Round Is Slightly Different

On your last cycle, the pattern changes in one respect: after inhaling and exhaling as usual and then holding the breath, instead of holding for a fixed count like 10 seconds, you hold for as long as you possibly can. After releasing that final hold, take a few minutes — around five — to simply relax and notice how you feel.

## Positioning and Practical Notes

You can do this practice seated or lying down. Standing is specifically discouraged, since it introduces problems (largely to do with the dizziness that can come with this kind of breathing). Sitting is the recommended position, though lying down is also fine.

## Safety Warnings

Two cautions are worth taking seriously:

- **Don't practice near water.** Because this breathing technique interacts with your blood pressure, there's a real chance of losing consciousness during or after the holds. Doing this near or in water turns that risk into a genuinely dangerous one.
- **If you have high blood pressure, think carefully before doing this.** The breathing pattern affects blood pressure directly, so it carries more risk for anyone in that situation. Approach it thoughtfully rather than diving straight in.

## Takeaways

- The Wim Hof Method has two components — breathing and cold (ice) exposure — and this practice covers only the breathing half.
- The core cycle: 30–40 rounds of deep inhale/passive release, followed by an exhale, a comfortable breath-hold, and a brief inhale-hold. Repeat for three to five cycles.
- The final cycle differs — hold the breath for as long as possible, then relax for a few minutes afterward.
- Practice seated or lying down, never standing.
- Avoid practicing near water, and if you have high blood pressure, proceed with real caution given the technique's direct effect on blood pressure.
