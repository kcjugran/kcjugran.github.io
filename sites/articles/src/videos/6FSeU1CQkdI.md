# A 5-Minute Breathing Technique to Calm Anxiety, No Medicine Needed

If your mind feels foggy, you tire quickly, or you just can't seem to focus when anxiety creeps in, there's a simple physical tool worth trying before reaching for anything else: a breathing technique that targets the sides of your rib cage. It takes only a few minutes, requires no equipment, and — according to the practitioner demonstrating it — can shift your mental and physical state almost immediately.

This is general information, not medical advice. If you're dealing with ongoing or severe anxiety, please talk to a qualified healthcare provider.

## Why Breathing Is the Starting Point

Anxiety doesn't just live in your thoughts — it shows up in your body, often as shallow, restricted breathing centered high in the chest. The technique described here works by deliberately expanding a part of your rib cage that most people never think to breathe into: the sides. The idea is that by consciously directing breath into this area, you interrupt the tight, shallow breathing pattern that anxiety tends to create, and give your nervous system a clear, physical signal to settle down.

## Getting Into Position

Before trying the breath itself, posture matters. The recommended setup is:

- Sit with your spine straight — cross-legged on the floor works well.
- Lying down is also an option if sitting isn't comfortable.
- Avoid doing this standing up, at least when you're starting out, since the technique takes some practice to feel. Once you're familiar with it, you can eventually do it standing too.

Getting the position right removes unnecessary tension from the body, so your attention can go entirely to the breath.

## Finding the Right Spot on Your Rib Cage

This technique is precise about where you're breathing from. Locate the lower portion of your rib cage — just below your chest, where you can feel the ribs on the sides of your torso. Rest your hands there, just below chest level, resting against your sides.

From this position, you're going to breathe in a way that expands the ribs sideways, not just the front of the chest. As you inhale, you should feel the ribs on each side pushing outward against your hands — a sideways stretch, rather than the usual up-and-down movement most of us associate with breathing.

## The Breathing Pattern

Here's the technique step by step:

1. **Inhale slowly**, directing the breath into the sides of your rib cage. Focus your attention on the sensation of your chest and ribs expanding sideways.
2. **Don't fill your lungs to full capacity.** Only breathe in about a quarter to a third of your full capacity — just enough to feel a gentle stretch in the chest and ribs, not a strained or forced one. If inhaling further starts to feel like effort, that's your cue to stop; you've gone too far.
3. **Hold briefly**, comfortably filling that space without straining or forcing anything further.
4. **Exhale slowly**, taking roughly double the time you spent inhaling. Let the elasticity of your chest and rib cage guide the release, allowing the ribs to settle back in naturally as the air leaves.

The emphasis throughout is on ease, not effort. You're not gasping for a big breath — you're working with a small, controlled volume of air and paying close attention to the physical sensation as it moves.

## Why the Short Exhale-Extension Matters

The instruction to make your exhale roughly twice as long as your inhale is a deliberate detail, not an incidental one. A longer exhale relative to inhale is a well-known way to engage the body's calming response — it's a simple ratio you can apply without needing to count seconds precisely, just aim for the exhale to noticeably outlast the inhale.

## What to Expect

Even a short session — the video suggests as little as 30 seconds of practice — can produce a noticeable shift in how you feel, both mentally and physically. The technique is described as something you can return to any time your mood needs resetting, whether you're feeling anxious, foggy, tired, or simply want to feel calmer and more focused.

It may feel a little complicated on a first read, since there are several steps to coordinate — hand placement, sideways expansion, partial-capacity inhale, extended exhale. That's normal. Watching a demonstration once or twice, or simply practicing slowly and patiently, is enough to get the hang of it.

## Takeaways

- The technique focuses on breathing into the **sides of the lower rib cage**, not just the front of the chest.
- Sit with a straight spine (or lie down) — avoid standing until you've practiced the technique a few times.
- Place your hands on your sides, just below the chest, to feel the ribs expand sideways as you inhale.
- Inhale only to about a quarter or a third of your full lung capacity — enough for a gentle stretch, not strain.
- Exhale slowly, aiming for roughly double the length of your inhale.
- Even a brief session, around 30 seconds, may bring a noticeable sense of mental and physical calm.
