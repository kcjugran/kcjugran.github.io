# Finding Your Life Purpose, Part 2: The 5-Question Daily Journal Method

In Part 1 of this series, Karan worked through the idea of life purpose itself — why it's a slippery, human-made concept rather than a fixed cosmic assignment, and why that's freeing rather than depressing. If you haven't done the Ikigai exercise from that video yet, do it first. This second part picks up where that one left off and won't make much sense without it.

Here, the focus shifts from philosophy to practice. Once you've done the one-time Ikigai reflection, you need something to return to daily — a set of questions sharp enough to cut through noise every morning. That's this method: five journal prompts, about five minutes each, repeated until your answers stop changing. When they stop changing, you've found something real.

## Why Values Matter More Than "Purpose"

Quick recap from Part 1: instead of hunting for one grand, cosmic purpose, look at what you already value. Values are the things you do without needing motivation — what you gravitate toward without anyone pushing you. A kid who plays video games for eight hours unprompted but can't sit still for twenty minutes of studying is showing you exactly where his values sit. Nobody bribes him to play games. Someone has to bribe or threaten him to study.

The higher something sits in your real values, the less motivation it takes to do it. The lower it sits, the more external pressure you need — money, guilt, fear of consequences. A useful diagnostic follows: watch what you actually spend time, money, and energy on, not what you claim to care about. Someone who says money matters but never saves is lying to themselves. A house full of books signals someone who values education; a home full of sports gear signals someone who values fitness. The evidence is already sitting around you.

The five daily questions are built to extract your real, lived values rather than your theoretical ones.

## The Five Clarity Questions

Set a 5-minute timer for each question. Don't overthink, don't edit yourself, don't judge what comes out. Just write.

**1. The One Crore Question**
Imagine your savings and investments are already handled — that part is done. Now you have one crore rupees (or your local equivalent) and exactly one year to spend it. Where does it go? Break it down category by category — fitness, food, travel, a business idea, sending money home, charity — until the full amount is accounted for. This forces concrete thinking about what you'd actually prioritize if resources weren't the constraint.

**2. Magic Wishes**
A genie appears and grants you any wish, no limits on realism. Write down everything that comes to mind — wanting to be taller, wanting to look a certain way, wanting to be famous, wanting effortless wealth. Don't filter for what's sensible. The point isn't literal wish fulfillment; it's noticing the shape of your desires without self-censorship.

**3. Risky Reward**
List things you know carry real risk but also real upside — asking someone out, quitting a stable job to travel, starting a company, taking a solo trip you're scared of. These are the moves where fear and desire sit side by side. Writing them down isn't a commitment to act; it's noticing what your mind keeps circling back to despite the risk.

**4. Biggest Pain**
List what's actually hurting in your life right now — strained relationships, financial insecurity, health you're unhappy with, a job that's fine except for a bad boss, an environment that wears you down. Be specific rather than diplomatic.

**5. Six Months, One Plan**
This one works differently. First, rank your answers to the previous four questions and take the top three from each — twelve points total: your real priorities, wishes, risk-reward bets, and pain points, ranked.

Now ask: if you had only six months to resolve or meaningfully improve these twelve things — or at minimum build a real plan for them — what would you actually do? The video's example: given six months to fix a health problem, you wouldn't keep watching random YouTube videos and asking relatives for advice — you'd hire the best expert you could afford. Apply that urgency to each point. For things that can't be fully solved in six months, like a full career change, the goal shifts to starting the process — enrolling in a course, having the first real conversation, taking the first concrete step.

## How to Actually Run This

Do all five questions in one sitting, roughly five minutes each — about 25 minutes total. Do this daily for at least two weeks. Don't look back at what you wrote the previous day before starting; write fresh each time.

That "don't look back" rule is what makes the method work. In the first several days, answers will shift noticeably — different points depending on your mood or what happened that day. By the second week, something changes: the same points start showing up again and again, almost unprompted. When that repetition sets in, you've found your actual values — not what you think you should value, not what your parents told you, but what your mind keeps returning to on its own.

Once your answers stabilize, add one line each day: given everything you've just written, what's the one practical action you can take today? Not a plan, not research — an actual step. Make a call, buy a course, quit something, start something. Do that one thing, then come back the next day and repeat.

## Practical Takeaway

- Complete the Ikigai exercise from Part 1 first — this builds directly on it.
- Set a 5-minute timer per question, all five in one sitting, without editing yourself.
- Repeat daily for at least two weeks; don't review yesterday's answers before writing today's.
- Watch for repetition — when the same points keep surfacing, that's your real value system showing itself.
- Once answers stabilize, close each session by naming one concrete action for that day, and take it.

Done consistently, this isn't a one-time insight exercise — it's a habit that sharpens your sense of direction over weeks, until the plan in your head stops being vague and starts being something you can act on.
