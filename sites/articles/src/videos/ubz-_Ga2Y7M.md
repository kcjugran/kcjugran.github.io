# Why You Have Low Back Pain — and How to Start Fixing It

Low back pain can come from many places — poor sleep posture, nutrient deficiencies, infections, or other medical issues. Those causes matter, but they're not the focus here. This article covers the most common everyday driver of low back pain: muscular imbalance.

## The Real Culprit: Muscular Imbalance

Your spine doesn't stand up and move on its own — it's held in place and guided by a ring of muscles surrounding your torso and hips: the front (abdominals), the back (spinal erectors), and the sides (obliques and hip muscles). When these muscles work together in a coordinated, balanced way, your spine is well supported and moves smoothly.

The problem starts when that coordination breaks down. Some muscles get weak. Some get tight or "stuck." Some simply aren't firing when they should. When this balance is off, the muscles around your spine can't properly support your lower back anymore, and over time, that lack of support shows up as chronic pain.

Think of it like driving a car with misaligned wheels — it doesn't track straight, it pulls and vibrates, and uneven pressure builds on parts of the system that were never meant to carry it. Your body works the same way: when the muscles around your core and hips are imbalanced, your lower back takes on load it shouldn't have to.

The good news: because this is a muscular and postural issue, it's something you can actively work on — through targeted stretching and mobility work that brings those muscles back into better alignment.

## Three Things to Keep in Mind Before You Start

Before jumping into the movements, keep these principles in mind — they matter as much as the exercises themselves:

1. **Form over speed.** Every exercise has a specific technique and style for a reason. Rushing through it or doing it "your own way" increases your risk of getting hurt and lowers your chances of actual results. Watch closely, follow the instructions, and prioritize doing it right over doing it fast.
2. **Follow the order.** The routine is sequenced deliberately — each movement builds on and complements the one before it. If you're following this as a full lower-back routine, go through it start to finish, in order.
3. **Listen to your body.** If any movement causes pain (not just mild stretch discomfort, but actual pain), stop that specific exercise. Try a lighter variation, and if that still doesn't feel right, skip it altogether. Nobody knows your body better than you do — trust your own judgment over pushing through discomfort.

## The Movements

**Breathing / decompression drill.** Lying down, the goal is to expand your ribcage in every direction as you inhale — front, back, and sides — rather than just puffing out your belly. Actively feel the back and sides of your ribcage widening as you breathe in, while keeping your abdomen engaged rather than letting it balloon outward. This 360-degree breathing pattern helps decompress the spine and activate the deep core muscles that support your lower back — a small, subtle movement, but a genuinely important one once you get the pattern.

**Hip circles / hip mobility drill.** Standing with knees softly bent, rotate your hips in a controlled circular pattern — pushing the hips back, rotating to one side, then forward, then to the other side. This mobilizes the hip and lower back area together and helps loosen tension that builds up around the pelvis. You can use your hands for light support if balance is an issue.

**Half-kneeling side stretch with rotation.** From a half-kneeling position (one knee down, one foot forward), reach overhead and lean to the side, letting your hips push slightly forward, then add a gentle rotation. This targets the side body and hip flexor on the down-knee leg — areas that get tight from prolonged sitting and feed lower back tension. Fold forward for more intensity, or keep a straighter posture if you're newer to this.

**Dead bug.** Lying on your back, extend opposite arm and opposite leg away from your body while keeping your lower back pressed flat into the floor, then return and switch sides. This is a core-stability exercise that teaches your midsection to control movement without your lower back arching or straining to compensate — one of the most valuable patterns for people with lower back pain, since it directly targets the "pocket" of instability that causes the pain.

**Bird dog.** On all fours, extend the opposite arm and leg simultaneously, keeping your hips level and stable throughout — no rocking side to side. The instruction here is important: keep the hips square and controlled; don't let the movement become a twisting or swaying motion. Extend the working leg back and up, foot flexed, keeping the motion clean and controlled rather than kicking upward.

**Side-lying leg lift.** Lying on your side, the bottom leg stays straight while the top leg lifts and moves back and up in a controlled arc — knee driving back toward the chest, then extending long. Keep the hips stable throughout; this is a hip and glute-focused movement that also supports the lower back indirectly by strengthening the muscles that stabilize the pelvis.

## Takeaways

- Most everyday lower back pain (when it isn't due to a separate medical issue) comes from muscular imbalance — some muscles too tight, others too weak or under-active — which leaves your lower back under-supported.
- The fix isn't one exercise; it's realigning the whole system of muscles around your spine and hips through consistent, correctly-performed mobility and stability work.
- Prioritize technique over speed or reps — poor form increases injury risk and reduces results.
- Do the movements in sequence rather than randomly, especially if you're using this as a full routine.
- If any exercise causes pain, stop, try a lighter variation, or skip it. Don't force it.
- Consistency matters more than intensity. Relaxed, steady, proactive effort over time is what resolves chronic tension — not one intense session.
