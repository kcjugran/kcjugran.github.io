# Working Out With Lower Back Pain: How to Train Smart Without Making It Worse

Lower back pain doesn't have to mean the end of your training. It usually means it's time to change *how* you train, not whether you train at all. The goal: keep making progress while giving the painful area a real chance to calm down and heal.

These are general training ideas, not medical advice — they come from coaching experience, not a diagnosis of your specific situation. If you have real pain or a suspected injury, talk to a doctor or physical therapist before changing your program. Treat what follows as a menu of ideas to discuss and experiment with, not a replacement for professional guidance.

## Reduce Bending, Stay More Upright

The key concept is the moment arm. Hold something close to your body and it feels light; hold the same object far away and it feels much heavier — same weight, more leverage working against you.

Your lower back works the same way. The more you bend forward, the further your torso's weight moves from your body's center point, and the more load your lower back resists. Stand more upright, and that load sits closer to center, so your lower back does far less work.

Practically: pick variations with less forward bend for a while. Instead of a Romanian deadlift, where you hinge deeply every rep, try a sumo-stance deadlift — the wider stance naturally keeps your torso more upright. Instead of a regular back squat, a front squat holds you more vertical too. This isn't about avoiding bent-over positions forever, just leaning on upright variations while things are irritated, since they tend to feel noticeably easier on the lower back.

## Try Unilateral or Lateral Variations

A second tip: instead of exercises where both legs hinge together (a standard deadlift), try staggered-stance or lateral variations — one leg forward and one back, or stepping side to side.

When one leg is forward or back, or you're moving laterally, the hip tends to lock into a more neutral position with some rotational freedom. Many people find this changes how the movement loads the spine, and the lower back feels noticeably less pressure than in the standard version. It doesn't work for everyone, but when it does, it works very well.

## Reduce the Load

If you're working with heavy weights, it may be time to back off for a while: drop the weight, increase your rep ranges, and stay away from training to failure.

Think of it like someone recovering from illness — you don't hand a sick person a full workload, you give them a lighter one and let them build back up gradually. Lighter loads still get blood flow moving and let your tissues recover and adapt, without the heavy strain. Once things feel better, bring the weight back up gradually.

## Use Isometric Holds

Isometrics — holding a position instead of moving through reps — are one of the safer ways to keep training a muscle without pushing it through a painful range of motion.

Take a Romanian deadlift: instead of bending and standing up repeatedly, go down into the hinge position and hold it — 10, 20, or 30 seconds at a time. You can only hold a position that long with a lighter load, so isometrics naturally keep the weight light while still giving the muscle a real training stimulus in that position. They're especially useful early in a comeback, when things are still sensitive.

## Train the Muscles Around the Injury

If your regular compound exercises trigger pain, train the surrounding muscles directly through isolation work, since your hips are closely connected to your lower back.

Examples: glute bridges (held or repped), side-lying leg raises for the glute medius, inner-thigh work, side planks, back extensions, and regular planks. None load the lower back the way a hinge movement does, but strengthening what's around it means that when you return to normal training, those supporting muscles are stronger — and you can do things you couldn't do before.

## Stretching — Used Selectively

Stretching shouldn't eat up half your workout, but a few targeted stretches can genuinely help:

- **Rotational stretch**: feet wide, hips pushed back like a hinge, both hands on an elevated surface — one hand presses down while the other reaches up and rotates open. Hold 10–20 seconds. Stretches the inner thigh and rotates the spine.
- **"Pancake" forward fold**: wide stance, folding forward with the stretch in the inner thighs, plus a sideways version that also stretches the obliques and QL (muscles along the side of the spine).
- **90/90 (pigeon) stretch**: front leg bent at 90 degrees, back leg also bent at 90 degrees, folding forward over the front leg. Targets the glutes and outer thigh/IT band. Hold 30–60 seconds per side.

Experiment and see which give your lower back genuine relief.

## When Nothing Else Works: Rest

If you've tried every variation and the pain still isn't budging, it may be time to rest the area completely. Hard advice for anyone who loves training, but sometimes a body part gets so irritated that the only real relief is leaving it alone. A one-to-three week break from directly loading that area — while still training other body parts, like upper body or lunge work that keeps your back straight — can be exactly what lets the pain settle.

## Takeaways

- Favor upright variations (sumo deadlifts, front squats) over deep-bending ones.
- Try unilateral or lateral stance variations if bilateral hinges trigger pain.
- Lower your training load and rep-to-failure intensity, then rebuild gradually.
- Use isometric holds to train a muscle without the strain of full repetitions.
- Strengthen surrounding muscles — glutes, inner thighs, obliques, core — through isolation work.
- Add a few targeted stretches (rotational, pancake fold, 90/90) for relief.
- If nothing helps, give the area a genuine 1–3 week rest and train around it.

General training information, not a substitute for diagnosis or a personalized treatment plan — check with a doctor or physical therapist before major changes if pain is persistent or severe.
